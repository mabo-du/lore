# Enhancing Oral History Diarization: A Decision Framework for Integrating CPU-Friendly Audio-Visual Active Speaker Detection

## Feasibility and Architecture of a CPU-Constrained ASD Pipeline

The integration of Audio-Visual Active Speaker Detection (ASD) into Lore's audio-first diarization pipeline is contingent upon the feasibility of constructing a complete, end-to-end system using open-source components that operate efficiently on consumer-grade CPUs with strict memory constraints of 8GB or less. The analysis of available research and model architectures confirms that such a pipeline is not only possible but can be realized using a modular stack of specialized lightweight models combined with standard optimization techniques. The core challenge lies in balancing model accuracy with computational efficiency, a problem addressed directly in recent developments in computer vision and deep learning deployment. The viability of this approach rests on three pillars: the selection of micro-scale models for face detection and tracking, the use of efficient neural network architectures for ASD, and the application of industry-standard optimization protocols like ONNX Runtime and INT8 quantization.

The foundational step in any video-based ASD system is the accurate and rapid detection of human faces within each video frame. For a CPU-constrained environment, this necessitates the use of highly optimized models. The provided materials point unequivocally towards **Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB** as a prime candidate [[16](https://gitee.com/yangpei123/Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB/blob/master/README.md?skip_mobile=true), [117](https://blog.csdn.net/weixin_45250844/article/details/106161829)]. This model is explicitly designed for edge computing devices and mobile applications, boasting a remarkably small file size of just 1.04 MB and a low computational footprint of 90 MFlops at a 320x240 input resolution [[16](https://gitee.com/yangpei123/Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB/blob/master/README.md?skip_mobile=true), [118](https://cloud.tencent.com/developer/article/1523648)]. Its key advantage is the direct support for ONNX export, which facilitates its deployment in environments where GPU acceleration is unavailable [[118](https://cloud.tencent.com/developer/article/1523648)]. While more powerful detectors like YOLOv8 variants offer higher accuracy, they often come with a heavier computational burden; however, lightweight adaptations such as GCS-YOLOv8 have been developed specifically for tasks like deepfake detection, demonstrating the trend towards smaller, faster models [[101](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.04605v1), [113](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/24/21/6781)]. Another YOLOv7-based detector was proposed to improve robustness against occlusion and small-scale faces, addressing some of the known failure modes of YOLO models [[60](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/16/8/3738), [112](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12845961/)]. However, for the stated goal of minimal resource consumption, Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB represents the most direct and efficient solution, offering a balance between speed and adequate detection capability for typical interview scenarios [[117](https://blog.csdn.net/weixin_45250844/article/details/106161829)].

Once faces are detected, a Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) algorithm is required to maintain consistent identities (`face_id`) across consecutive frames. This is critical for linking a speaker's lip movements in one frame to their head pose in the next, especially when speakers move, turn away, or are temporarily occluded. Older tracking systems like DeepSORT rely heavily on motion cues and appearance features, but more recent advancements have significantly improved robustness. Algorithms such as **BoT-SORT** and **LITE** represent a paradigm shift in MOT [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.04187v1), [18](https://developer.aliyun.com/article/1226958)]. BoT-SORT enhances traditional SORT by incorporating ReID features, camera motion compensation, and a more sophisticated association strategy based on IoU and ReID distances [[18](https://developer.aliyun.com/article/1226958)]. LITE builds upon classic DeepSORT to achieve high accuracy at 28.3 FPS on the challenging MOT17 benchmark, making it exceptionally fast and suitable for real-time CPU processing [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.04187v1)]. These modern trackers are superior to older methods because they incorporate low-confidence detections and employ more advanced ReID models, which helps prevent ID switches during brief occlusions—a common failure mode in group settings [[19](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Comparison-of-the-performances-of-BYTE-and-SORT-under-different-detection-score_fig1_355237366), [20](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/13285931779)]. The ability of these trackers to handle temporary loss of visual information is crucial for maintaining track integrity in the dynamic environments typical of oral history recordings [[61](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2026/papers/Li_Occlusion-Aware_SORT_Observing_Occlusion_for_Robust_Multi-Object_Tracking_CVPR_2026_paper.pdf), [102](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12167898/)]. Their efficiency makes them ideal components for a pipeline designed to run on modest hardware.

The final and most computationally intensive stage is the Active Speaker Detection (ASD) model itself, which classifies whether a given tracked face is speaking. While academic benchmarks are often set by complex models like TalkNet, which achieves high accuracy on datasets like AVA-ActiveSpeaker, these models are generally too large for real-time CPU inference [[8](https://build.nvidia.com/nvidia/active-speaker-detection/modelcard), [11](https://opendatalab.com/OpenDataLab/AVA-ActiveSpeaker)]. Therefore, the focus must be on lightweight alternatives. Research explicitly proposes several such architectures, including **LR-ASD**, a lightweight and robust network constructed by reducing the number of input candidates and optimizing convolutional layers [[37](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389991138_LR-ASD_Lightweight_and_Robust_Network_for_Active_Speaker_Detection), [86](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2023/html/Liao_A_Light_Weight_Model_for_Active_Speaker_Detection_CVPR_2023_paper.html)]. Other studies aim to create efficient end-to-end frameworks by splitting 2D and 3D convolutions and redesigning the fusion mechanism to reduce redundancy [[10](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2023/papers/Liao_A_Light_Weight_Model_for_Active_Speaker_Detection_CVPR_2023_paper.pdf), [14](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/A-Light-Weight-Model-for-Active-Speaker-Detection-Liao-Duan/3856061231c298f77477f08f9c314e9e594ed485)]. NVIDIA has also developed a lightweight architecture for its ASD model, featuring separate audio and visual encoders and a gated concatenation fusion layer to improve efficiency [[8](https://build.nvidia.com/nvidia/active-speaker-detection/modelcard)]. The enabling technology that makes these lightweight models practical for CPU deployment is **quantization** and the use of **ONNX Runtime**. Quantization, particularly post-training quantization to 8-bit integers (INT8), is a well-established technique for reducing model size, memory footprint, and inference latency, which is critical for on-device and edge deployments [[53](https://quark.docs.amd.com/latest/tutorials/onnx/accuracy_improvement/smooth_quant/onnx_smooth_quant_tutorial.html), [135](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.02118v1)]. The ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) format provides a standardized way to represent machine learning models, allowing them to be exported from frameworks like PyTorch and executed efficiently on various hardware [[42](https://docs.pytorch.org/docs/stable/onnx_export.html), [45](https://www.aidoczh.com/torchtutorials/advanced/super_resolution_with_onnxruntime.html)]. ONNX Runtime has been shown to provide better inference performance than native frameworks on certain Intel CPUs, further enhancing the case for this deployment strategy [[64](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2303.05016)]. The availability of pre-quantized models, such as the Conformer-Transducer INT8 models provided by the Sherpa-ONNX project for streaming ASR on edge devices, serves as a strong proof-of-concept for this entire pipeline architecture [[128](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9709/12/4/107)]. By combining a micro-detector, a robust modern tracker, and a quantized lightweight ASD model, all deployed via ONNX Runtime, a complete and feasible ASD pipeline can be assembled entirely from open-source components.

## Performance Benchmarks and Hardware Requirements

Determining the precise performance of a composite pipeline composed of multiple distinct models requires synthesizing data from individual component analyses, as no single source provides end-to-end benchmarks for this exact configuration. However, based on the characteristics of the recommended lightweight models and general principles of CPU-based inference, it is possible to establish reasonable estimates for hardware requirements, RAM consumption, and achievable frame rates. These estimates are crucial for validating that the proposed ASD pipeline can operate effectively within Lore's specified constraints of running on a CPU with ≤8GB of total system RAM and without blocking the parallel audio transcription process.

The minimum viable hardware for this pipeline consists of a modern consumer-grade central processing unit (CPU). Processors from the last five years with at least four cores and strong multi-threaded performance are sufficient. A representative baseline would be an **Intel Core i5-1135G7** or an **AMD Ryzen 5 5500U** [[131](https://www.cpubenchmark.net/compare/3830vs4141/Intel-i5-1135G7-vs-AMD-Ryzen-5-5500U), [132](https://versus.com/en/amd-ryzen-5-5500u-vs-intel-core-i5-1135g7)]. The AMD Ryzen 5 5500U, for instance, is noted to be around 35% faster in multi-threaded testing compared to the Intel Core i5-1135G7, making it a slightly more capable choice for demanding workloads [[131](https://www.cpubenchmark.net/compare/3830vs4141/Intel-i5-1135G7-vs-AMD-Ryzen-5-5500U)]. Desktop-class CPUs would offer even greater performance, potentially enabling higher frame rates or supporting multiple streams concurrently [[89](https://www.hp.com/us-en/workstations/zbook-fury.html)]. The key requirement is not raw clock speed alone but strong integer performance and efficient multi-core handling, as these are the primary drivers for the operations performed by the lightweight models and the ONNX Runtime. Memory bandwidth is also a factor, though the overall RAM footprint is kept low by the use of quantized models.

RAM consumption is a critical constraint, capped at 8GB for the entire system. The memory usage for the proposed ASD pipeline will be dominated by the loaded model weights and the video frame buffer. Based on the model sizes and typical overheads, the RAM requirement per concurrent video stream can be estimated as follows:
*   **Face Detector:** The Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB model is approximately 1MB in size, but loading it and managing its execution context might require closer to **50-100 MB** of RAM [[16](https://gitee.com/yangpei123/Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB/blob/master/README.md?skip_mobile=true), [109](https://dev.to/george_mbaka_62347347417a/tiny-ai-models-for-raspberry-pi-to-run-ai-locally-in-2026-ik1)].
*   **Tracker:** Models like BoT-SORT or LITE have moderate memory requirements. Assuming a few hundred megabytes for the ReID feature extractor and associated state variables, a conservative estimate would be **~300 MB** [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.04187v1), [18](https://developer.aliyun.com/article/1226958)].
*   **ASD Model:** Even a lightweight, quantized ASD model like a reduced version of TalkNet or LR-ASD would likely occupy **~1-1.5 GB** of RAM once loaded [[8](https://build.nvidia.com/nvidia/active-speaker-detection/modelcard), [37](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389991138_LR-ASD_Lightweight_and_Robust_Network_for_Active_Speaker_Detection)].
*   **Pipeline Overhead & Frames:** The ONNX Runtime, Python interpreter, and storage for a small queue of video frames (e.g., 10-20 frames) would add another **~100-200 MB**.

Adding these together, the total RAM consumption for a single video stream would be approximately **1.9 GB to 2.1 GB**. This leaves a substantial amount of the 8GB limit available for Lore's primary audio processing pipeline (pyannote.audio, Resemblyzer), operating system functions, and other processes. This calculation suggests that the pipeline is well within the specified memory budget. One study involving a Raspberry Pi 5, which typically offers up to 8GB of RAM, reported stable memory usage below 1GB and CPU spikes under 50% across 4 cores during inference, providing a useful anecdotal reference for the efficiency of such optimized pipelines [[109](https://dev.to/george_mbaka_62347347417a/tiny-ai-models-for-raspberry-pi-to-run-ai-locally-in-2026-ik1), [134](https://community.ultralytics.com/t/raspberry-pi-5-crash-when-running-yolov11n-ncnn/1525)].

The overall frame rate of the pipeline is determined by its slowest component. Given the lightweight nature of the selected models, achieving a throughput of **10 to 20 frames per second (FPS)** on a modern consumer CPU is a realistic expectation for a single video stream. This range is derived from the following synthesized estimates for each stage's inference time:
*   **Face Detection (Ultra-Light):** At 320x240 resolution, the model has a computational cost of 90 MFlops [[118](https://cloud.tencent.com/developer/article/1523648)]. On a modern CPU, this translates to a very fast inference time, estimated to be between **5 and 10 milliseconds per frame**.
*   **Tracking (BoT-SORT/LITE):** These algorithms involve feature extraction, Kalman filtering, and association calculations. Their high-speed design allows them to operate at **~28 FPS** on the MOT17 benchmark, suggesting an inference time of roughly **10 to 20 milliseconds per frame** [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.04187v1)].
*   **ASD (Quantized Model):** This is the most computationally expensive step. A quantized lightweight model like LR-ASD would take longer than detection or tracking, with an estimated inference time ranging from **30 to 100 milliseconds per frame**, depending on the number of active tracks being processed simultaneously.

With these times, the total latency per frame would be in the range of 45-130 ms, comfortably allowing for a frame rate of 10-20 FPS. This is more than sufficient for accurately detecting speaker turns, as it far exceeds the temporal resolution needed for diarization. Critically, this level of performance ensures that the video processing can run in a parallel, non-blocking fashion relative to the audio transcription pipeline, fulfilling a core requirement of the research goal. The low CPU utilization (e.g., <50% across 4 cores) observed in similar embedded AI applications further reinforces the conclusion that this workload is manageable on the target hardware [[134](https://community.ultralytics.com/t/raspberry-pi-5-crash-when-running-yolov11n-ncnn/1525)].

## Contextual Value of ASD for Oral History Scenarios

The decision to integrate an Audio-Visual Active Speaker Detection (ASD) pipeline into Lore's system hinges on a clear understanding of its value proposition within the specific contexts of oral history recording. The benefits of ASD are not uniform across all scenarios; rather, they are highly dependent on the number of participants, the structure of the conversation, and the acoustic environment. An analytical evaluation reveals that while ASD offers marginal utility in traditional, structured interviews, its value becomes significant and often indispensable in unstructured, multi-speaker group settings. This distinction is fundamental to designing a judicious implementation that applies ASD where it provides the most benefit, thereby justifying its computational cost.

In the most common oral history scenario—a one-on-one interview between an interviewer and an interviewee—the added value of an ASD pipeline is limited. In these settings, the "who spoke when" problem is largely resolved by the context of the interview itself. The person being interviewed is typically the sole individual on camera, while the interviewer may be off-camera, static, or occasionally visible [[81](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.26177v1)]. Audio-only diarization systems, such as those built with the `pyannote.audio` toolkit, are highly effective at distinguishing between two speakers based on their vocal characteristics [[73](https://pypi.org/project/pyannote-audio/2.1.1/), [95](https://hal.science/hal-04247212v1/document)]. The primary ambiguity, if any, arises from short overlaps or moments of simultaneous speech, which are less frequent in dyadic conversations compared to group discussions. For these scenarios, the computational overhead of running a full video pipeline is likely unjustified when the existing audio-first method provides a sufficiently accurate result. The system's default behavior should remain the robust audio-only diarization, as it is simpler, faster, and consumes fewer resources.

The true potential of ASD is unlocked in scenarios involving three or more participants. This includes community panel discussions, family history gatherings, or group reminiscence sessions, which are increasingly common in community-based oral history projects [[33](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2201.06841)]. In these situations, audio-only diarization systems face significant challenges. The primary issue is speech overlap, where multiple people speak at the same time, creating ambiguous audio signals that are difficult to de-mix and assign to individual speakers [[69](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/24/3950)]. Studies on challenging audio datasets like DIHARD-III show that the average speech overlap ratio can exceed 30%, making robust overlap detection and resolution a critical task for improving diarization error rates (DER) [[126](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/354220583_Scenario-Dependent_Speaker_Diarization_for_DIHARD-III_Challenge)]. Purely acoustic methods struggle to determine who initiated a turn or who was speaking over whom in such chaotic environments [[56](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/4/2002)]. Here, ASD provides a powerful, complementary cue. By correlating lip movements with audio energy, the system can visually identify the active speaker, resolving ambiguities that are insurmountable for audio alone [[92](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335829350_Who_Said_That_Audio-Visual_Speaker_Diarisation_of_Real-World_Meetings)]. For example, if two people are talking over each other, the ASD model can indicate which of the two visible faces is actually moving its lips, providing a strong signal to the diarization system. Datasets designed to test these capabilities, such as MISP-Meeting, are specifically created to reflect real-world meeting scenarios with an average of 5.55 speakers per recording, highlighting the relevance of this multi-speaker context [[123](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.13971), [139](https://challenge.xfyun.cn/misp_dataset)]. The successful application of multimodal cues in such settings has been shown to dramatically improve downstream tasks like Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR), reducing character error rates from over 36% to below 21% by isolating the target speaker's voice [[114](https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.753/)]. This cleaner, more focused audio signal inherently benefits the speaker clustering and segmentation stages of diarization.

Even within the seemingly simple 1-on-1 interview format, there are intermediate cases where ASD can provide a valuable boost in accuracy. If the recording environment is acoustically poor—for instance, with high levels of background noise, echo, or reverberation—the audio signal can become unreliable. Similarly, if the interviewee and interviewer have very similar-sounding voices, or if the interviewee speaks with a heavy accent or disfluent speech, the audio embeddings used by systems like Resemblyzer may become unstable [[3](https://www.scribd.com/document/828772162/resemblyzer), [4](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373481646_Speaker_Identification_Differentiation_and_Verification_Using_Deep_Learning_for_Human_Machine_Interface)]. In these noisy or ambiguous conditions, the visual confirmation from ASD can act as a tie-breaker. It can help the system confidently assign a speaker label during periods of silence or when the acoustic signal is momentarily lost. This makes ASD a powerful tool for improving robustness in less-than-ideal recording conditions, which are prevalent in field recordings conducted in homes or community centers rather than professional studios. Therefore, the justification for using ASD is strongest in unstructured, multi-speaker environments where its unique ability to resolve spatial and temporal ambiguities in overlapping speech provides a decisive advantage over purely acoustic methods. Its role is not to replace the excellent audio-first diarization for dyadic interviews, but to serve as a targeted enhancement for the more challenging, yet equally important, multi-participant scenarios found in oral history archives.

## Robustness and Fallback Strategies for Realistic Conditions

While the theoretical benefits of Audio-Visual Active Speaker Detection (ASD) are compelling, its practical utility in the domain of oral history is critically dependent on its robustness under the varied and often suboptimal recording conditions typical of community-based projects. Unlike controlled laboratory settings, real-world video recordings frequently suffer from low resolution, poor and inconsistent lighting, partial facial occlusions, and speakers moving out of frame or turning away from the camera [[26](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2590123025032268), [28](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3801962)]. These factors can severely degrade the performance of both the face detection and ASD components of the pipeline. Consequently, a successful integration into Lore cannot assume perfect visual data; it must incorporate a sophisticated fallback strategy that gracefully degrades the system's reliance on the visual modality when it becomes unreliable.

The challenges facing computer vision models in realistic scenarios are well-documented. Low-resolution video, a common issue with webcams or mobile phone recordings, reduces the quality of facial features, making detection and tracking more difficult [[26](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2590123025032268)]. Variable and poor lighting can introduce biases into detection models, causing them to fail or produce incorrect bounding boxes [[84](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389842260_Impact_of_Lighting-Based_Biases_on_the_Performance_of_YOLOv8_Object_Detection_Models)]. Extreme lighting conditions, such as backlighting or shadows, can obscure facial details entirely [[136](https://arxiv.org/html/2508.11834v1)]. Facial occlusions, caused by hands, hair, glasses, or objects, present another major hurdle. A study on target active speaker detection noted that even with lip occlusion, a robust model could still perform, but this remains a significant challenge for many systems [[87](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373247890_Target_Active_Speaker_Detection_with_Audio-visual_Cues)]. Similarly, multi-object tracking (MOT) algorithms struggle when objects are temporarily lost due to occlusion, leading to identity switches or lost tracks [[61](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2026/papers/Li_Occlusion-Aware_SORT_Observing_Occlusion_for_Robust_Multi-Object_Tracking_CVPR_2026_paper.pdf), [62](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.06034)]. The AVA-ActiveSpeaker dataset itself was designed to include examples of occlusions and low-quality audio to better reflect real-world difficulties [[59](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2303.04439)]. These documented failure modes mean that any ASD pipeline integrated into Lore must be treated as a probabilistic system, not a deterministic one.

To mitigate these issues, a combination of robust model selection and intelligent software-level strategies is necessary. First, the choice of models themselves plays a crucial role. As previously discussed, modern MOT algorithms like BoT-SORT and LITE are designed to be more resilient to temporary occlusions by leveraging long-term spatio-temporal predictions and robust ReID features [[18](https://developer.aliyun.com/article/1226958), [102](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12167898/)]. Similarly, newer ASD architectures are being explicitly designed for robustness. For example, UniCon is presented as an efficient framework for robust ASD, while LASER uses lip landmark assistance to improve performance [[99](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.11899), [111](https://vipl.ict.ac.cn/people/syang/202205/P020220524764636141848.pdf)]. Some research proposes auxiliary consistency losses that align predictions from different modalities (e.g., lip-aware vs. face-only) to handle missing or corrupted data, which could be invaluable in Lore's context [[36](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/WACV2026/html/Nguyen_LASER_Lip_Landmark_Assisted_Speaker_Detection_for_Robustness_WACV_2026_paper.html)]. Second, a comprehensive fallback strategy must be implemented at the software level. This strategy should monitor the health and confidence of the entire pipeline and switch to a safer mode of operation when visual data becomes unreliable. This can be achieved through a series of checks:

1.  **Confidence Thresholding:** Each ASD model prediction should come with a confidence score. If the average confidence across all active speaker tracks drops below a predefined threshold (e.g., 70%), the system should deem the visual evidence unreliable and ignore the ASD output for that segment of the video, reverting to the audio-only diarization.
2.  **Tracking Stability Monitoring:** The MOT algorithm should report its own health metrics, such as the percentage of faces successfully tracked and the number of ID switches. If the tracking becomes unstable—for instance, if more than 30% of tracks are lost or there are frequent ID switches—the system should disable the ASD pipeline for that period and fall back to the audio-only results.
3.  **Pre-processing Quality Checks:** Before initiating the full pipeline, a preliminary analysis of the video could flag problematic conditions. If the video is identified as having consistently low resolution, severe motion blur, or prolonged periods of extreme occlusion, the system could bypass the ASD processing altogether, saving computational resources and avoiding erroneous outputs.
4.  **Modality Failure Handling:** In line with research on handling missing modalities, the system should be designed to function correctly even when one modality (the visual one) is absent or degraded [[35](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.00861v1)]. The presence of a video file should trigger the *option* to run ASD, not force it. The primary diarization path should always be the robust audio-only pipeline.

This multi-layered fallback strategy ensures that the introduction of the ASD module does not compromise the overall reliability of Lore's diarization service. Instead of being a brittle dependency, the visual component becomes an optional, context-aware enhancer. When video quality is good and the scene is conducive to analysis (e.g., a clear view of multiple speakers' faces), ASD provides a powerful signal to resolve ambiguities. When conditions are poor, the system intelligently defaults to its proven audio-based methods, ensuring that users always receive a usable diarization result, albeit potentially with lower accuracy in challenging overlap scenarios. This principled approach to handling failure is essential for building a trustworthy tool for archivists and researchers who depend on accurate metadata for historical documents.

## Integration Strategy within Lore's Diarization Ecosystem

Integrating the Audio-Visual Active Speaker Detection (ASD) pipeline into Lore's existing audio-first ecosystem requires a carefully designed strategy that complements, rather than replaces, the current `pyannote.audio` and Resemblyzer backends. The primary goals are to leverage the strengths of the visual modality to correct ambiguities in the audio-only output, ensure the video processing runs in parallel without blocking transcription, and map the novel visual data into Lore's established internal and external data structures. A fusion or post-correction layer architecture emerges as the most robust and effective approach, positioning ASD as a "confidence booster" for the primary diarization engine.

The optimal operational flow involves parallel execution of the audio and video pipelines. Upon receiving a video file as input, Lore would initiate two independent processing threads. The first thread would execute the standard audio-only diarization pipeline, processing the video's audio track to generate an initial set of speaker turns, which would be formatted according to standards like Rich Transcription Time Marked (RTTM) [[40](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/23/4/2082), [96](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373247993_pyannoteaudio_21_speaker_diarization_pipeline_principle_benchmark_and_recipe)]. Simultaneously, the second thread would run the newly proposed CPU-based ASD pipeline: face detection, object tracking, and active speaker classification. This parallelism is critical to meet the user's requirement that video processing must not block the audio transcription pipeline. The estimated frame rate of 10-20 FPS for the ASD pipeline on a modern CPU ensures it can keep pace with the video stream without introducing significant delays [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.04187v1), [118](https://cloud.tencent.com/developer/article/1523648)].

Once both pipelines have completed their respective analyses, the integration logic begins. The output of the ASD pipeline is fundamentally different from the output of the audio diarizer. The audio pipeline produces a sequence of time-stamped segments, each assigned a speaker label (e.g., `SPEAKER_A`, `SPEAKER_B`) based on acoustic embeddings. The ASD pipeline produces a sequence of time-stamped events indicating which tracked face IDs are currently active [[44](https://arxiv.org/html/2305.12831v3)]. To bridge this gap, a mapping mechanism must be established. Each unique `face_id` generated by the Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) algorithm corresponds to a specific speaker track. This `face_id` can be temporarily mapped to a placeholder speaker label within Lore's internal diarization object, for example, `VISUAL_SPEAKER_1`. This allows the system to correlate visual activity with the timeline of the audio recording.

The core of the integration is a fusion algorithm that combines the acoustic evidence from the audio diarizer with the visual evidence from the ASD pipeline. This fusion should not be a simple overwrite but a nuanced decision-making process based on confidence scores from both modalities. The following logic can be implemented:
1.  **Baseline Agreement:** For timestamps where the audio diarizer is confident about a speaker label and the ASD pipeline indicates that the corresponding visual track is inactive, the audio result stands as the ground truth.
2.  **Ambiguity Resolution:** When the audio diarizer is uncertain (e.g., a sudden change in speaker embedding with no clear voice activity boundary), but the ASD pipeline provides a high-confidence active speaker label, the visual evidence takes precedence. This is particularly useful for resolving turn-taking ambiguities in overlapping speech.
3.  **Contradiction Handling:** If the outputs from the two modalities conflict—for example, the audio suggests speaker A is talking, but the ASD shows speaker B's face is active—the system must decide how to proceed. A conservative approach would be to flag this discrepancy for potential manual review or to give priority to the more reliable modality based on contextual factors (e.g., trusting audio more in noisy conditions where lip-reading is hard). A simpler fallback is to retain the audio-only result in case of disagreement.
4.  **Confidence-Based Weighting:** A more advanced fusion model could weight the contributions of each modality by its respective confidence score. If both are highly confident and agree, the final label is assigned a high certainty. If only one is confident, its label is used. If neither is confident, the system falls back to its default behavior.

This fusion approach transforms ASD from a standalone diarizer into a powerful refinement tool. It leverages the high accuracy of audio-only methods in clear conditions while using the unique spatiotemporal information from video to break ties and resolve ambiguities that are otherwise intractable. The final output of the system would remain in Lore's standard formats (e.g., RTTM), but the underlying data structure would be enriched with information derived from the visual analysis where it was beneficial. This ensures backward compatibility and seamless integration into Lore's existing workflows, providing archivists with a more accurate and robust diarization product without requiring them to learn a new system or data format.

## Synthesis and Decision Framework for Implementation

The comprehensive analysis confirms that integrating a lightweight, open-source Audio-Visual Active Speaker Detection (ASD) pipeline into Lore's audio-first system is a technically feasible and strategically sound initiative. The resulting system holds the potential to significantly enhance speaker diarization accuracy for a specific and growing subset of oral history recordings: those involving three or more speakers in unstructured, group settings. However, this enhancement comes with computational costs and inherent limitations related to visual data quality. Therefore, a successful implementation depends not only on technical execution but also on the deployment of a thoughtful decision framework that activates the ASD pipeline judiciously and employs a robust fallback strategy to maintain system reliability.

The core of the proposal is a modular, CPU-friendly pipeline comprising three key components: a highly optimized face detector like **Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB** [[16](https://gitee.com/yangpei123/Ultra-Light-Fast-Generic-Face-Detector-1MB/blob/master/README.md?skip_mobile=true)], a robust multi-object tracker such as **BoT-SORT** [[18](https://developer.aliyun.com/article/1226958)], and a lightweight ASD model like **LR-ASD** [[37](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389991138_LR-ASD_Lightweight_and_Robust_Network_for_Active_Speaker_Detection)], all deployed via **ONNX Runtime** after being optimized with **INT8 quantization** [[53](https://quark.docs.amd.com/latest/tutorials/onnx/accuracy_improvement/smooth_quant/onnx_smooth_quant_tutorial.html), [64](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2303.05016)]. This architecture is projected to operate efficiently on modern consumer CPUs, consuming approximately 2-3 GB of RAM per stream and achieving a frame rate of 10-20 FPS, thus satisfying the strict hardware constraints. The primary value of this pipeline is its ability to resolve ambiguities caused by overlapping speech—a common failure point for purely acoustic diarization systems like `pyannote.audio` [[69](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/24/3950), [126](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/354220583_Scenario-Dependent_Speaker_Diarization_for_DIHARD-III_Challenge)]. Its utility is maximized in multi-speaker scenarios typical of community panels and family history gatherings, where audio-only methods falter. Conversely, its value in traditional 1-on-1 interviews is marginal, as the context and acoustic differences are usually sufficient for accurate diarization.

However, the effectiveness of this visual modality is contingent on the quality of the source video. Factors such as low resolution, poor lighting, and facial occlusions can severely degrade performance, a reality common in informal oral history recordings [[26](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2590123025032268), [28](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3801962)]. A naive implementation that forces the use of a potentially unreliable visual pipeline would risk lowering the overall accuracy of Lore's service. Therefore, the proposed integration strategy positions ASD as a conditional enhancer, not a mandatory component. It operates in parallel with the main audio pipeline and its output is used to refine, rather than replace, the baseline audio diarization. This is achieved through a confidence-based fusion logic that weighs the evidence from both modalities, prioritizing the audio-only result when the visual data is deemed untrustworthy.

To manage this trade-off systematically, the following decision framework is recommended for triggering and managing the ASD pipeline:

| Condition Category | Trigger Criteria | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Activation Conditions** | **Number of Speakers:** Estimated ≥ 3 from initial audio analysis. | High speaker count increases probability of ambiguous overlaps where ASD excels [[139](https://challenge.xfyun.cn/misp_dataset)]. |
| | **Speech Overlap Ratio:** Detected > 30% from audio analysis. | High overlap is a primary failure mode for audio-only diarization; ASD can resolve these ambiguities [[126](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/354220583_Scenario-Dependent_Speaker_Diarization_for_DIHARD-III_Challenge)]. |
| | **Environmental Noise:** Ambient noise level is above a defined threshold. | Poor acoustic signal makes visual cues more valuable for disambiguation. |
| **Fallback Conditions** | **Low ASD Confidence:** Average model confidence score < 70%. | Visual evidence is unreliable; revert to the more robust audio-only pipeline. |
| | **Tracking Instability:** MOT algorithm loses > 30% of tracks or experiences frequent ID switches. | Unstable tracking leads to incorrect speaker associations; visual data is unusable [[61](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/CVPR2026/papers/Li_Occlusion-Aware_SORT_Observing_Occlusion_for_Robust_Multi-Object_Tracking_CVPR_2026_paper.pdf)]. |
| | **Poor Visual Quality:** Pre-processing detects low resolution, extreme motion blur, or persistent occlusions. | Degraded video quality prevents accurate face and lip analysis [[87](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373247890_Target_Active_Speaker_Detection_with_Audio-visual_Cues), [100](https://escholarship.org/content/qt84s4n7hq/qt84s4n7hq_noSplash_cb3b53fb61becb972ff185ce3be726c0.pdf)]. |

In summary, the integration of a lightweight ASD pipeline is a justified and valuable enhancement for Lore. It equips the platform to handle the increasing diversity of video-based oral history recordings with greater accuracy and nuance. By adopting the recommended modular architecture, performance expectations, and, most importantly, the conditional decision framework, Lore can deploy this advanced feature in a manner that is both powerful and reliable. The system will deliver state-of-the-art results in challenging multi-speaker scenarios while gracefully maintaining its proven performance in simpler, 1-on-1 interviews, ultimately providing archivists and researchers with a more versatile and accurate tool for organizing historical audiovisual content.
