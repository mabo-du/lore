# Lightweight Flagging, Full Attribution Later: An Incremental Strategy for Overlapping Speech Diarization in CPU-Constrained Lore Applications

## State-of-the-Art Open-Source Diarization Models for Overlap Handling

The core challenge facing Lore is the inability of its current diarization pipeline, based on `pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1`, to handle overlapping speech [[8](https://modelscope.cn/models/pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1)]. In oral history recordings, where interruptions, backchannels ("mhm"), and crosstalk are common structural elements, this limitation results in a significant loss of conversational context [[22](https://community.openai.com/t/diarization-only-detects-one-speaker-issue/1360817), [32](https://ticnote.com/en/blog/best-medical-transcribing-software)]. The research objective requires identifying modern, open-source solutions capable of addressing this issue natively. The analysis of available technologies points unequivocally to the evolution of the `pyannote.audio` toolkit as the primary avenue for progress. Unlike its predecessor, which was designed around a single-speaker-per-segment paradigm, recent versions of `pyannote.audio` have been architected from the ground up to support overlap-aware speaker diarization.

A critical architectural innovation enabling this capability is the reformulation of the diarization task itself. Historically, overlapped speaker diarization has often been treated as a complex multi-label classification problem, which can struggle with speaker dependency and accurate identification in highly overlapping environments [[2](https://aclanthology.org/2022.emnlp-main.505.pdf), [3](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/24/3950)]. However, more recent research, integrated into the `pyannote.audio` framework, proposes a more elegant solution by reframing the task as a single-label prediction using "power set encoding" (PSE) [[4](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/365613622_Speaker_Overlap-aware_Neural_Diarization_for_Multi-party_Meeting_Analysis)]. This technique allows each time segment to be assigned a label corresponding to a subset of all possible speakers. For example, a segment containing two speakers would be labeled with a unique identifier representing the pair, rather than being forced into a multi-label output. This shift simplifies the learning objective and has proven effective in improving performance on challenging conversational data [[4](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/365613622_Speaker_Overlap-aware_Neural_Diarization_for_Multi-party_Meeting_Analysis)]. This fundamental change in how the model processes the audio stream is what enables the native resolution of simultaneous speech.

The practical application of these advancements is embodied in specific, pretrained models released under the `pyannote.audio` umbrella. One such model, `DialogueDub`, explicitly demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on conversational corpora while being inherently aware of overlaps [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)]. The development of `DialogueDub` represents a significant step forward, achieving a Diarization Error Rate (DER) of 8.3% on the AMI meeting corpus and 9.1% on the VoxConverse dataset [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)]. These benchmarks are particularly relevant for Lore, as both the AMI Meeting Corpus and the VoxConverse dataset are composed of naturally occurring, multi-party conversations that include many of the phenomena found in oral history, such as interruptions and rapid-fire exchanges [[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.04809v1), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.09226v1)]. The AMI corpus, in particular, consists of 100 hours of meeting recordings annotated at multiple levels, providing a rich resource for training and evaluation [[27](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228341280_The_AMI_meeting_corpus), [38](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-AMI-Meeting-Corpus%3A-A-Pre-announcement-Carletta-Ashby/e4e0fd56309e28b28bb47c9a72ad6111c76bb8b9), [39](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Wessel-Kraaij/publication/228341280_The_AMI_meeting_corpus/links/0fcfd51276fca4cec3000000/The-AMI-meeting-corpus.pdf)]. The fact that `DialogueDub` outperforms baseline systems that ignore diarization (and thus have effectively 100% speaker confusion) underscores its robustness [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)].

Further evidence of the `pyannote.audio` ecosystem's maturity comes from its modular design and continuous improvement. The toolkit provides a suite of neural building blocks for constructing end-to-end diarization pipelines [[41](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/1911.01255), [67](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Pyannote.Audio%3A-Neural-Building-Blocks-for-Speaker-Bredin-Yin/8b5bcba6794dc3e95d8235640396ca9e0c2634f2)]. A typical modern pipeline involves several stages: Voice Activity Detection (VAD) to isolate speech regions, extraction of speaker embeddings (fingerprints) for each speaker, and finally, a segmentation/clustering stage that assigns speakers to segments [[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.26177v1), [54](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/373247993_pyannoteaudio_21_speaker_diarization_pipeline_principle_benchmark_and_recipe)]. Recent work has focused on enhancing the final segmentation stage to better handle overlaps. For instance, one study proposed modifying the statistics pooling layer to give less weight to frames where the segmentation model predicts simultaneous speakers, thereby making the subsequent clustering more robust to acoustic confusion during overlaps [[75](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Overlap-Aware-Low-Latency-Online-Speaker-Based-on-Coria-Bredin/eeddc26f17f2ed10b47da2f1d390333437b60480)]. Another key component is the use of powerful self-supervised speech representations, such as WavLM-base, which have been shown to be highly effective for downstream tasks like diarization [[58](https://hal.science/hal-04969566/document), [74](https://hal.science/hal-04969548v1/file/2023___pyannote_audio___VoxSRC_2023.pdf)]. The integration of these advanced components, such as layer 8 of WavLM-base, has led to a 7% relative DER improvement on the VoxConverse test set, demonstrating the tangible benefits of leveraging cutting-edge pre-trained models [[74](https://hal.science/hal-04969548v1/file/2023___pyannote_audio___VoxSRC_2023.pdf)].

While `pyannote.audio` stands out as the leading open-source solution, it is important to acknowledge the broader landscape of speaker diarization research. Some approaches attempt to solve the problem by integrating diarization with Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) into a single, end-to-end system, such as WhisperX-Anchored Pyannote or SpeakerLM [[16](https://aclanthology.org/2024.clib-1.3.pdf), [55](https://arxiv.org/html/2508.06372v1), [61](https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/AAAI/article/view/40745/44706)]. While promising, these systems can be complex to implement and may not offer the modularity that Lore might require. Other research explores hybrid methods, such as guiding online diarization with a separate speech separation module [[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2402.00067v1)], or even using large language models (LLMs) to post-correct diarization errors [[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2406.04927v3)]. However, for the immediate goal of adding overlap awareness, the direct, out-of-the-box capabilities of `pyannote.audio`'s latest models provide the most direct and well-supported path forward. The existence of a dedicated "overlapped-speech-detection" model within the same ecosystem further reinforces its suitability, offering a potential lightweight solution for the initial phase of the project [[53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)]. This model has been used to identify overlapped speech regions in various datasets, including those with explicit overlapping segments, validating its utility for the task [[29](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12648582/), [53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)].

In summary, the state-of-the-art in open-source diarization has matured beyond the single-speaker assumption. Toolkits like `pyannote.audio` now provide robust, pretrained models like `DialogueDub` that are explicitly designed for overlap-aware diarization. Their proven performance on relevant conversational datasets and their modular architecture make them the ideal candidates for upgrading Lore's capabilities. The transition from the legacy `speaker-diarization-3.1` model to a modern `pyannote.audio` pipeline is not merely an upgrade but a fundamental enhancement that directly addresses the core technical deficit in handling the complex, overlapping nature of spoken conversation.

| Feature | Legacy System (`pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1`) | Modern System (`DialogueDub` via `pyannote.audio`) |

This comparison clearly illustrates the generational leap in capability offered by modern open-source tools, positioning `pyannote.audio` as the definitive choice for implementing overlap-aware diarization in Lore.

## Computational Feasibility and Resource Management on CPU

The primary operational constraint for Lore is its requirement to run entirely offline on a CPU, with a strict total memory budget of less than 8GB RAM. This constraint significantly impacts the feasibility of deploying modern, deep-learning-based diarization models, which are computationally intensive. The analysis must therefore focus on whether a full overlap-aware pipeline can be executed within this resource envelope and explore strategies for managing the associated computational costs. The good news is that while challenging, running such a pipeline on a CPU is plausible, especially when leveraging optimized inference frameworks and adopting a strategic, phased implementation approach.

The central question revolves around the RAM consumption of the neural network models and the overall system load. Large transformer-based architectures, such as WavLM-base, which are used as backbones in state-of-the-art diarization systems, have a substantial memory footprint just for their weights [[74](https://hal.science/hal-04969548v1/file/2023___pyannote_audio___VoxSRC_2023.pdf)]. Loading such a model into RAM will consume hundreds of megabytes, if not over a gigabyte, of memory. When combined with the operating system, the Python runtime, the audio processing buffers, and the concurrent execution of other heavy applications like Whisper, NLLB, and GLiNER, the risk of exceeding the 8GB limit is very real. While some studies quantify GPU memory usage—for instance, noting that inference for Pyannote took approximately 16GB of VRAM on an Nvidia T4—it does not provide a clear picture of the total system RAM required for a comparable CPU-only setup [[47](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.03945)]. Therefore, any decision to replace the current engine must be preceded by careful profiling of the entire application stack to establish a firm baseline and understand the headroom available for new components.

Despite this challenge, there are well-established pathways for optimizing CPU-based inference to make it practical. The user's specification to use PyTorch CPU, ONNX, or CTranslate2 is correct and aligns with best practices for high-performance local AI applications. ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) is an open format for representing machine learning models that allows them to be run efficiently across different frameworks and hardware backends [[63](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexander-lukashov_github-foxnosetechdiarize-speaker-diarization-activity-7434207012395319296-84C4)]. Converting a `pyannote.audio` model to ONNX format can unlock significant performance gains on CPU by enabling optimizations that are not present in the native PyTorch execution path. Similarly, CTranslate2 is a highly optimized translation tool for neural networks that is known for its speed and efficiency on CPU, offering performance improvements of up to 7x over standard libraries in some cases [[63](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexander-lukashov_github-foxnosetechdiarize-speaker-diarization-activity-7434207012395319296-84C4)]. Several sources confirm the viability of using these tools for fast, local speaker diarization, making them essential components for meeting Lore's performance goals [[10](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/78820971/pyannote-load-and-apply-speaker-diarization-offline), [63](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexander-lukashov_github-foxnosetechdiarize-speaker-diarization-activity-7434207012395319296-84C4)]. By utilizing these optimization techniques, the computational cost of running a full `DialogueDub`-level model can be reduced to a level that is potentially acceptable for a modern desktop CPU, though it will still be slower than GPU-accelerated inference [[47](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.03945)].

Given the user's strong preference for a lightweight overlap flagging solution as a near-term goal, a hybrid, two-stage approach emerges as a strategically sound and risk-mitigated strategy. This approach directly addresses the RAM constraints by minimizing the computational overhead of the initial processing. The `pyannote` ecosystem includes a dedicated "overlapped-speech-detection" model, which is specifically designed to identify regions of simultaneous speech [[53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)]. This model is likely to be smaller and far less computationally demanding than a full end-to-end diarization system. Its purpose is singular: to output a timeline of overlap events. Because of its specialized function, it consumes fewer resources, fitting comfortably within the 8GB RAM budget.

This leads to a pragmatic workflow: first, run the lightweight overlap detector on the audio file. If the detector finds no overlaps, the system can proceed with the existing, highly efficient `speaker-diarization-3.1` pipeline, which is already part of the Lore stack. This represents the common case where audio is relatively clean. Only if the detector flags a region of overlap would the system need to invoke the heavier, multi-speaker-aware `DialogueDub` model to perform full attribution for that specific segment. This conditional execution model dramatically reduces the frequency with which the most resource-intensive process needs to run. It ensures that the majority of user files processed by Lore can continue to benefit from the fast, low-resource fallback, while reserving the powerful overlap-resolving capabilities for the minority of files where they are truly needed. This tiered approach perfectly balances the user's desire for immediate value (flagging) with the long-term goal of full attribution, all while staying within the strict resource limitations.

The following table estimates the RAM and CPU impact for different implementation approaches, keeping in mind that actual values will depend on the specific hardware and audio content length.

| Implementation Approach | Estimated RAM Impact (per process) | Estimated CPU Impact | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Baseline (Current)** | Low (existing engine + Resemblyzer) | Low-Medium | Uses established, lightweight models. |
| **Phase 1: Lightweight Detector** | Very Low (<100 MB) | Very Low | A small, specialized model for detecting overlap [[53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)]. |
| **Phase 2: Full Multi-Speaker Resolver** | High (500 MB - >1 GB for model) | High | Requires loading a large transformer-based model (e.g., WavLM) and performing complex computations [[74](https://hal.science/hal-04969548v1/file/2023___pyannote_audio___VoxSRC_2023.pdf)]. |
| **Conditional Execution (Flagging then Resolve)** | Low (Baseline) + Very Low (Detector) | Low (Baseline) + Very Low (Detector) | The most resource-efficient path. The heavy resolver is only loaded when necessary, mitigating peak RAM usage. |

In conclusion, while running a state-of-the-art, overlap-aware diarization model on CPU within an 8GB RAM budget is a significant engineering challenge, it is not insurmountable. The use of optimized inference formats like ONNX and CTranslate2 is critical for achieving acceptable performance [[63](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexander-lukashov_github-foxnosetechdiarize-speaker-diarization-activity-7434207012395319296-84C4)]. However, the most prudent and effective strategy is the incremental adoption pathway. By first implementing a lightweight overlap detector, Lore can deliver immediate value while carefully managing its resource consumption. This approach directly supports the user's 70/30 weighting towards flagging, serves as a proof-of-concept, and builds a foundation for eventually introducing the full multi-speaker resolver in a controlled and efficient manner.

## Data Model Evolution for Representing Overlapping Speech

To properly support the detection and resolution of overlapping speech, Lore's internal data model must evolve beyond its current single-speaker-per-segment structure. The existing `Segment` model, which assumes non-overlapping time ranges and a single `speaker_label`, is fundamentally incompatible with representing simultaneous utterances [[8](https://modelscope.cn/models/pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1)]. Attempting to force overlapping speech into this model would lead to data loss, inaccuracies, and a brittle system architecture. The user's strategic decision to prioritize a clean, forward-looking internal data model over rigid compatibility with the OHMS XML 6.0 export schema is a crucial design choice that will pay dividends in the long term. This approach decouples the core logic of the application from the limitations of an external export format, allowing Lore to support advanced features internally without compromise.

The proposed forward-looking data model should be simple, intuitive, and semantically precise. Instead of a single `speaker_label`, a new type of object, tentatively named `OverlapSegment`, should be introduced. This object would represent a contiguous time interval `[start_ms, end_ms]` and associate it with a *set* of speakers who were active during that interval. For example, a segment could have a `speaker_set = {'interviewee_1', 'interviewer'}`. This representation cleanly captures the essence of the "who spoke when?" question, even when the answer is not a single person. For non-overlapping segments, the `speaker_set` would simply contain one element, maintaining backward compatibility with the old logic in spirit if not in form. This dual-use capability makes the transition smoother, as the same underlying concept can handle both simple and complex cases.

This model is trivial to implement from a software engineering perspective. It primarily involves defining a new class or data structure with attributes for `start_ms`, `end_ms`, and `speaker_set`. The changes to the `DiarizationEngine` abstraction would be minimal at first, as it would just need to learn how to produce `Segment` objects (for non-overlap) and `OverlapSegment` objects (for overlap). The complexity shifts from the core engine to the downstream consumers of the data—such as the UI display and LLM summarization modules—which would need to be updated to interpret the `speaker_set` attribute. This is a manageable refactoring task compared to the alternative of warping the entire internal data structure to fit the export format. Treating OHMS XML 6.0 compliance as a secondary translation or post-processing concern is the correct separation of concerns. When the time comes to generate an OHMS-compliant file, a dedicated export module can take the rich internal representation and map it onto the fixed schema as lossily or losslessly as required.

Several strategies can be employed for this translation layer. For an `OverlapSegment` containing two speakers, the export module could:
1.  **Flatten the Segment:** Create two separate, non-overlapping segments in the output file, one for each speaker. This is lossy, as it erases the simultaneity information, but it preserves the timeline.
2.  **Create a Special Annotation:** Add a special annotation or flag to the transcript that marks the period as an overlap, perhaps without assigning a single speaker. This preserves the structural information at the cost of detailed speaker attribution in that region.
3.  **Omit Detailed Speaker Info:** Retain the original timeline and text but omit the fine-grained speaker labels for the overlapping period, replacing them with a generic tag like `<OVERLAP>`.
4.  **Use Nested Segments (if schema allows):** Although OHMS 6.0 is a fixed schema, some export formats allow for nested structures. If this is permissible, an `OverlapSegment` could be represented as a parent segment containing two child segments, each with a speaker label. This would be the most faithful representation but depends on the specific export tool's capabilities.

By decoupling the internal model from the export format, Lore retains maximum flexibility. It can choose the best representation internally and adapt its export strategy based on user feedback or evolving standards. This future-proofs the application, ensuring that as diarization technology advances and users demand more sophisticated features, the core data model will not become a bottleneck. The decision to build a robust internal model first is a hallmark of sound software architecture, and it is strongly recommended for Lore's next phase of development. The proposed `OverlapSegment` with a `speaker_set` is a powerful yet simple abstraction that directly enables the desired functionality and lays a solid foundation for future enhancements.

| Aspect | Current Model (Single-Speaker) | Proposed Model (Multi-Speaker) | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Core Concept** | Represents a timeline of individual speakers. | Represents a timeline of events, where an event can involve one or more speakers. | More accurately reflects the reality of human conversation. |
| **Time Representation** | Non-overlapping `start_ms` / `end_ms` ranges. | Allows for overlapping time intervals. | Essential for capturing simultaneous speech. |
| **Speaker Attribute** | Single `speaker_label` string. | `speaker_set` containing strings. | Semantically correct for representing multiple participants in an event. |
| **Internal Logic** | Simple one-to-one mapping of time to speaker. | Can handle one-to-one and one-to-many mappings. | Provides the necessary flexibility for overlap-aware diarization. |
| **OHMS Export** | Direct mapping is possible but loses overlap info. | Requires a translation/adapter layer to conform to the fixed schema. | Decouples the powerful internal model from the constraints of the export format. |

This comparison highlights the necessity of the data model evolution. The proposed model is not just an incremental tweak; it is a foundational change that unlocks the ability to meaningfully process and represent overlapping speech, which is central to the user's research goal.

## Incremental Adoption Pathway: From Flagging to Full Attribution

Adopting a full, multi-speaker-aware diarization system is a significant undertaking. To manage risk, ensure steady progress, and deliver value to users early, an incremental adoption pathway is the most strategic approach. This roadmap leverages the available technologies in a logical sequence, starting with a minimal-effort, high-value solution and progressively building towards a more comprehensive feature set. This aligns perfectly with the user's stated preference for prioritizing overlap flagging (the 70%) over full multi-speaker attribution (the 30%).

**Phase 1: Implement a Lightweight Overlap Flagging Mechanism (Minimum Viable Product)**

The immediate goal is to address the core user pain point: the silent failure of the diarization pipeline on overlapping audio. The most direct way to do this is to add a lightweight overlap detector that simply identifies and flags these problematic regions. The `pyannote` ecosystem provides exactly this capability through its "overlapped-speech-detection" model [[53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)]. This model is designed for this specific purpose and is computationally inexpensive.

The implementation for this phase would involve creating a new method or stage within the `DiarizationEngine` abstraction. This stage would accept an audio file and, before passing it to the main diarization logic, would run the lightweight overlap detector. The output of this detector is a list of timestamps marking the beginning and end of overlapping speech segments. This information can then be fed back to the main application, which can annotate the corresponding text transcript. For example, the text for an overlapping segment could be wrapped in a special tag like `<OVERLAP_START>` and `<OVERLAP_END>`, or a boolean flag could be added to the segment object itself. This simple change delivers immense value: it tells the user that a section of the recording was acoustically complex and that the speaker labels in that region may be unreliable. This preserves structural information that is currently lost and provides immediate feedback without requiring a complete rewrite of the diarization backend.

From a resource perspective, this phase is exceptionally low-risk. The overlap detection model is small and fast, consuming minimal RAM and CPU cycles. It fits comfortably within the 8GB RAM constraint and adds negligible processing time to the overall workflow. This allows the team to validate the concept on real-world oral history audio without impacting the performance of the rest of the application. This MVP serves as a critical proof-of-concept, demonstrating that Lore can begin to "see" and report on overlapping speech.

**Phase 2: Integrate Full Multi-Speaker Attribution for Overlapping Regions**

Once the flagging mechanism is in place and proven, the next logical step is to move from merely flagging overlaps to resolving them. This phase fulfills the remaining 30% of the user's value proposition. The implementation here involves updating the `DiarizationEngine` to incorporate a second, more powerful diarization backend. The logic would be conditional: if Phase 1's overlap detector flags a segment, the system invokes the full `pyannote.audio` pipeline using a state-of-the-art model like `DialogueDub` [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)]. This model is capable of performing speaker-by-speaker transcription and diarization, even in highly overlapping scenarios [[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.20288v1)].

The output from `DialogueDub` will be a set of segments, some of which will be identified as containing multiple speakers. As discussed in the previous section, these outputs will populate the new `OverlapSegment` model with a `speaker_set` attribute. This provides the user with the complete answer to the "who spoke when?" question, even in the most complex conversational moments. This is the long-term vision for Lore's diarization capabilities.

The resource impact of this phase is higher. Invoking `DialogueDub` requires loading a much larger neural network model, which increases the RAM footprint, and performing more intensive computations, which increases the CPU time. However, because this model is only triggered conditionally for flagged segments, the peak resource consumption is managed. The system does not need to run the heavy-duty resolver on every file or even every segment of a file. This targeted approach keeps the overall resource usage within the bounds of feasibility for a modern desktop CPU with the 8GB RAM limit. The total RAM impact remains a concern, but it is a manageable one, especially with the use of optimized backends like ONNX or CTranslate2 [[63](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexander-lukashov_github-foxnosetechdiarize-speaker-diarization-activity-7434207012395319296-84C4)]. This phased approach allows the development team to profile the exact resource requirements of the full-resolution model in the context of the live application stack before committing to a full rollout.

This two-phase pathway offers a balanced and pragmatic route to achieving the project's goals. It delivers tangible user value in the short term with a low-cost, low-risk implementation and provides a clear, justified path toward the complete solution in the medium term. It respects the user's priorities, manages technical debt, and ensures that the application evolves in a stable and sustainable manner.

## Integration Strategy and Impact on the `DiarizationEngine`

The successful implementation of overlap-aware diarization necessitates modifications to Lore's core architecture, particularly the `DiarizationEngine` abstraction. The user has indicated that while the current abstraction is not "sacred," breaking it should only be done if the payoff is substantial. The analysis confirms that the proposed incremental pathway warrants such a change, as it will enable a cleaner, more modular, and ultimately more powerful system. The integration strategy should focus on creating a flexible pipeline that can accommodate different diarization backends and data models.

The current `DiarizationEngine` was designed around the synchronous output model of `pyannote` v3.x, which produced a flat list of non-overlapping segments [[8](https://modelscope.cn/models/pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1)]. This design is fundamentally incompatible with the goal of representing overlapping speech. A new abstraction must be able to handle asynchronous outputs and heterogeneous data types (i.e., both `Segment` and `OverlapSegment` objects). The recommended approach is to refactor the `DiarizationEngine` into a more modular pipeline. This pipeline would consist of distinct stages or processors that can be chained together.

For the incremental adoption pathway, this new pipeline could look like this:

1.  **Preprocessing Stage:** Handles audio input, ensuring it meets the required format (e.g., 16kHz mono) [[8](https://modelscope.cn/models/pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1)].
2.  **Detection Stage (Optional):** Runs the lightweight `pyannote.overlapped-speech-detection` model [[53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)]. This stage's output is a list of timestamped overlap regions.
3.  **Resolution Stage:** This is the heart of the engine and will contain the conditional logic.
    *   If the Detection Stage found overlaps, it passes the entire audio file to the **Heavy-Diarizer Backend**. This backend would instantiate and run the `DialogueDub` model from `pyannote.audio` [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)]. The output from this backend would be a collection of `OverlapSegment` objects, ready for the final stage.
    *   If the Detection Stage found no overlaps, it passes the audio file to the **Lightweight-Diarizer Backend**. This backend would invoke the existing `pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1` model or the Resemblyzer fallback. The output from this backend would be a collection of standard `Segment` objects.
4.  **Post-processing & Formatting Stage:** Takes the output from the Resolution Stage and formats it into a standardized internal representation that the rest of the application can consume. This stage would be responsible for converting the outputs from both backends into a consistent data structure, perhaps by wrapping them in a common base class or by having a unified return type from the Resolution Stage.

This pipelined architecture has several advantages. First, it cleanly separates concerns, making the code easier to understand, test, and maintain. Second, it is highly extensible. Adding a new detection or resolution model in the future would simply involve creating a new processor and plugging it into the pipeline, without needing to modify the core engine logic. Third, it directly implements the conditional execution strategy, which is key to managing resource consumption.

Breaking the old `DiarizationEngine` abstraction is therefore not a negative outcome but a necessary step toward a more robust and future-proof design. The new architecture is better aligned with modern machine learning workflows, which often involve chaining together multiple models and processing steps. It embraces the complexity of the real world—where sometimes you need a light touch and other times a heavy one—and provides a scalable framework for handling it. The refactoring effort will be significant but is a worthwhile investment that will pay off as Lore continues to evolve and integrate more advanced AI features. The improved modularity and clarity of the new system will also make it easier to onboard new developers and contribute to the project's long-term sustainability.

## Strategic Recommendations and Implementation Roadmap

Based on a comprehensive analysis of the available open-source technologies, computational constraints, and user priorities, this report provides a clear set of recommendations for equipping Lore with the capability to handle overlapping speech. The overarching strategy is to adopt a phased, incremental approach that delivers immediate value while paving the way for a more comprehensive solution. This path is designed to be technically feasible within the specified CPU and RAM constraints, architecturally sound, and maximally aligned with the user's stated goals.

The primary recommendation is to migrate from the legacy `pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1` model to the modern `pyannote.audio` toolkit, leveraging its state-of-the-art, overlap-aware models like `DialogueDub` [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)]. The performance of `DialogueDub` on conversational datasets such as AMI and VoxConverse provides strong evidence of its suitability for oral history audio [[23](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341084470_PyannoteAudio_Neural_Building_Blocks_for_Speaker_Diarization)]. The key to managing the significant resource demands of this migration lies in a two-stage implementation plan that prioritizes a lightweight overlap detector before committing to full multi-speaker resolution.

The following ranked list outlines the recommended implementation approaches, from minimal-effort to comprehensive, along with their estimated impacts and strategic value.

**Ranked List of Implementation Approaches**

1.  **Approach 1: Lightweight Overlap Flagging (High Priority MVP)**
    *   **Description:** Integrate the `pyannote.overlapped-speech-detection` model into the diarization pipeline [[53](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352340925008716)]. This model acts as a first-pass filter, identifying and annotating timestamped regions of overlapping speech in the transcript.
    *   **Estimated RAM/CPU Impact:** Very Low. The detector model is small and fast, posing minimal strain on system resources. It can be expected to operate well within the <8GB RAM budget.
    *   **Strategic Value:** This approach directly addresses the primary user pain point with the least amount of effort. It provides immediate, actionable feedback to the user, preserving crucial conversational structure that is currently lost. This aligns with the user's 70% weighting towards flagging and serves as a valuable proof-of-concept.
    *   **Recommended For:** Immediate implementation to demonstrate progress and gather user feedback.

2.  **Approach 2: Conditional Full Attribution (Long-Term Goal)**
    *   **Description:** Build upon Approach 1 by updating the `DiarizationEngine` to conditionally invoke a full multi-speaker diarization model (e.g., `DialogueDub`) only for the segments flagged as overlapping by the lightweight detector.
    *   **Estimated RAM/CPU Impact:** Medium-High. This involves loading and running a larger, more complex neural network. While still manageable due to conditional execution, it will increase RAM usage and processing time. Use of ONNX or CTranslate2 is highly recommended to optimize performance [[63](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexander-lukashov_github-foxnosetechdiarize-speaker-diarization-activity-7434207012395319296-84C4)].
    *   **Strategic Value:** This approach delivers the complete solution, answering not just "was there overlap?" but "who was speaking?". It fulfills the 30% of the user's value proposition related to full attribution and represents the long-term vision for Lore's diarization capabilities.
    *   **Recommended For:** A follow-up implementation after the success and stability of the flagging mechanism have been established.

3.  **Approach 3: Unified End-to-End Pipeline (High-Risk, High-Reward)**
    *   **Description:** Replace the entire diarization pipeline with a single, unified `pyannote.audio` pipeline that handles both non-overlapping and overlapping speech seamlessly from the start. This would involve a complete overhaul of the `DiarizationEngine` and the internal data model.
    *   **Estimated RAM/CPU Impact:** High. This approach runs the heavy-duty `DialogueDub` model on the entire audio file, leading to the highest possible RAM consumption and longest processing times.
    *   **Strategic Value:** Offers the most streamlined and theoretically optimal solution, avoiding conditional logic and potential edge cases in the pipeline.
    *   **Recommended For:** Consideration only if the conditional approach proves to be overly complex or if performance on average files becomes a bottleneck. Given the user's preference for efficiency, this is the lowest-priority option.

**Final Recommended Pathway**

The most prudent and effective course of action for Lore is to execute the incremental pathway outlined in Approaches 1 and 2.

*   **Step 1: Refactor `DiarizationEngine`** to support a pipelined architecture. This refactoring is essential for cleanly integrating the new models and data structures.
*   **Step 2: Implement the Lightweight Overlap Flagging** (Approach 1). This delivers immediate value and validates the core technology.
*   **Step 3: Update the Engine to Support the New Data Model** (`OverlapSegment` with a `speaker_set`). This creates the clean internal representation needed for future features.
*   **Step 4: Implement Conditional Full Attribution** (Approach 2). This completes the primary feature set, delivering both flagging and full attribution.

By following this roadmap, Lore can successfully evolve its diarization capabilities. It will move from a system that fails silently on overlaps to one that actively acknowledges and intelligently resolves them, thereby preserving the rich, nuanced structure of oral history conversations. This approach responsibly manages the significant technical and resource challenges, ensuring that the application remains stable, performant, and true to its user-centric goals.
