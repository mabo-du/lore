# **Overlapping Speech Detection in Diarization Pipelines: Edge-Compute Deployment Strategies for Oral History Applications**

## **The Architectural Challenge of Overlapping Speech in Oral History**

The transcription, annotation, and digital preservation of oral history represent uniquely complex challenges within the broader field of computational speech processing and computational linguistics. Unlike structured audio environments such as broadcast news, audiobooks, or formal dictation, oral history recordings are characterized by highly unstructured, spontaneous, and dynamically evolving conversational interactions. These acoustic dynamics inherently produce significant amounts of overlapping speech. In the context of linguistic analysis and historical preservation, this overlap is not merely acoustic noise or interference; it constitutes a critical layer of communicative data. Phenomena such as interruptions, cross-talk, collaborative sentence completions, and structural backchannels (for example, affirmative grunts, expressions of agreement like "mhm," or concurrent laughter) carry profound sociolinguistic meaning. They define the power dynamics, emotional resonance, and collaborative engagement between the interviewer and the interviewee.  
Historically, traditional speaker diarization systems—which operate under the mandate of determining "who spoke when"—have relied on single-speaker-per-timeline assumptions. These foundational models execute voice activity detection followed by clustering, forcing the algorithmic pipeline to assign every given segment of audio to one and only one dominant speaker. When two people speak simultaneously, the acoustic embeddings become entangled. In a monophonic timeline, these simultaneous acoustic events are processed destructively: the pipeline either arbitrarily assigns the overlapping segment to whichever speaker possesses higher acoustic energy at that millisecond, or it drops the secondary speech event entirely, viewing it as unclassifiable noise. The resulting loss of data strips the final transcript of its structural conversational metadata and significantly degrades the fidelity of the historical record.  
Deploying overlap-aware diarization pipelines is further complicated when operating under strict, edge-compute hardware constraints. Applications designed to run entirely offline on consumer-grade hardware—specifically, desktop environments constrained to central processing units (CPUs) with less than 8GB of total system RAM—face severe computational bottlenecks. Such systems cannot leverage the massive parallelization, high-bandwidth memory, and tensor core accelerations of dedicated Graphics Processing Units (GPUs). In these edge ecosystems, the diarization engine does not run in isolation. It must coexist and share resources with other highly memory-intensive models, including robust automatic speech recognition (ASR) engines like Whisper, translation models like NLLB, and named entity recognition architectures such as GLiNER.  
This exhaustive research report provides a comprehensive analysis of the current state of the art in open-source, overlap-aware speaker diarization. It rigorously evaluates the computational cost of these advanced neural models, analyzes their memory footprints to verify operational feasibility within a strict 8GB RAM CPU constraint, and proposes the specific data architectural shifts required to represent simultaneous speakers within a modern, Oral History Metadata Synchronizer (OHMS) XML 6.0 compatible data model. Finally, the report delineates a tiered, incremental implementation strategy to transition legacy transcription pipelines toward robust, edge-capable overlap detection without sacrificing systemic stability.

## **The State of the Art in Open-Source Overlap-Aware Diarization**

The open-source landscape for speaker diarization has undergone a paradigm shift, transitioning away from cascaded statistical systems that rely on simple threshold-based Voice Activity Detection (VAD) and moving toward sophisticated end-to-end neural pipelines capable of isolating and resolving multiple simultaneous speakers. The preeminent framework driving this evolution is pyannote.audio, an open-source toolkit built in Python that utilizes advanced frame-level multi-label classification to detect and process overlapping speech.

### **The Evolution from Pyannote 3.1 to the Community-1 Architecture**

The transition from the established pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1 pipeline to the newer pyannote.audio 4.x architecture, which is championed by the community-1 open-source model, represents a major milestone in accessible diarization technology.1 To understand the improvements, it is necessary to analyze the mechanics of the predecessor. The 3.1 architecture introduced highly robust local neural speaker segmentation utilizing short, heavily overlapping sliding windows. Specifically, the model typically applies a 5-second window that slides across the audio stream in 500-millisecond steps, capturing densely overlapping predictions.3 Within each window, the neural network analyzes every 16-millisecond frame and outputs independent probability scores for multiple potential speakers, explicitly trained to handle up to three simultaneous speakers (Kmax \= 3).3 Because the activation functions allow multiple speaker nodes to exceed the confidence threshold simultaneously, the pipeline possesses native Overlapped Speech Detection (OSD) capabilities.4  
However, the legacy 3.1 model frequently encountered limitations during the global clustering phase. While the local segmentation accurately identified that two voices were speaking at a specific frame, the downstream clustering algorithm—often utilizing Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering (AHC)—struggled to maintain consistent speaker identities over long temporal spans, leading to speaker confusion.6  
The community-1 pipeline addresses these specific deficiencies directly. It has established itself as the premier open-source solution by significantly outperforming the 3.1 legacy model across nearly all key metrics, specifically in the realm of global speaker assignment and long-term counting.1 The model dramatically reduces speaker confusion—defined as instances where transcribed speech is assigned to the incorrect identity—while maintaining the identical high-fidelity segmentation and overlapped speech detection performance that characterized the 3.1 release.2 This ensures consistent speaker identity tracking across entire conversations, a critical requirement for long-form oral history interviews.2

An in-depth analysis of these benchmark metrics reveals profound third-order insights into acoustic modeling performance. The AMI Meeting Corpus, consisting of 100 hours of multi-modal, highly interactive meeting recordings, is structurally highly representative of multi-speaker oral history environments where dynamic turn-taking occurs.1 The reduction in Diarization Error Rate from 18.8% to 17.0% on the AMI Individual Headset Microphone (IHM) split indicates that the community-1 model is inherently superior at untangling rapid conversational turns when the signal-to-noise ratio is favorable.1 However, the performance degradation observed on the Single Distant Microphone (SDM) split—yielding a 19.9% DER—suggests that far-field acoustics, room reverberation, and spatial distance remain persistent challenges when attempting to isolate overlapping speech.1  
Similarly, the VoxConverse dataset is highly instructive. Comprising multi-speaker clips extracted from YouTube political debates and news segments, the VoxConverse corpus was specifically curated to present a high density of overlapping speech and rapid, aggressive interruptions.1 The observation that the community-1 model plateaus at an 11.2% DER on VoxConverse—matching the performance of the 3.1 legacy model exactly—implies that the underlying neural architecture for the core *segmentation* phase (the mechanism responsible for identifying the precise frame boundaries of overlap) has reached a local optimum.1 The algorithmic improvements present in community-1 are therefore largely derived from superior clustering logic and global speaker assignment over extended durations, rather than a fundamental revolution in the local frame-level overlap detection mechanism itself.2  
The DIHARD 3 dataset, which includes highly challenging audio sources such as clinical interviews, restaurant conversations, and amateur web videos, serves as an absolute stress test.1 The slight improvement from 21.4% to 20.2% in community-1 demonstrates steady progress, but the relatively high overall error rate underscores that overlapping speech in noisy environments remains a frontier challenge for open-source systems.1 Furthermore, the lack of an explicitly cited ICSI benchmark in the most recent Pyannote publication literature indicates that while the models are tested on highly diverse conversational corpora, they are optimized primarily toward modern multi-party formats (like AliMeeting and Ego4D) rather than older, highly specific academic corpora.1

### **The Mechanics and Vulnerabilities of Overlap Detection**

To engineer a pipeline that operates effectively within severe hardware constraints, one must understand the precise vulnerability of the Overlapping Speech Detection (OSD) mechanism. In the pyannote ecosystem, OSD is not treated as an isolated, discrete downstream task. Instead, it is embedded natively within the foundational segmentation phase.8 The core segmentation model (such as pyannote/segmentation-3.0) outputs continuous multi-class probabilities for every 16-millisecond frame.5 By permitting the activation functions to yield multiple speaker probabilities exceeding the confidence threshold simultaneously, the model inherently identifies regions where two or more speakers are active.4  
The vulnerability of this architecture lies entirely in the downstream clustering stage. When two voices overlap, their acoustic features mix, causing the resulting neural embeddings to become highly entangled in the multi-dimensional latent space.6 Traditional clustering algorithms, particularly Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering (AHC) or MeanShift, attempt to force this entangled, blended embedding into a single distinct speaker cluster.3 A recent, exhaustive oracle component analysis of the Pyannote 3.1 pipeline revealed a striking statistical disparity: while overlapping speech accounts for only roughly 10% of total speech time in standard conversational datasets, it is directly responsible for nearly 50% of the total diarization errors.6  
The analysis demonstrated that the segmentation module itself is highly conservative; it tends to miss the edges of overlapping speech rather than falsely hallucinate non-existent speech. However, the clustering module experiences profound mathematical confusion when fed these entangled embeddings. Specifically, two-thirds of the total recorded speaker confusion was traced directly back to areas of overlap.6 While modern pipelines attempt to mitigate this by dynamically masking overlapping frames during the embedding extraction phase—preventing the contaminated frames from poisoning the pure speaker clusters—the pipeline still requires complex, computationally heavy heuristics to retroactively assign identities to the overlapping segments.10

### **The "Exclusive Mode" Paradox in Transcription Pipelines**

In an explicit attempt to reconcile the complexities of highly precise, multi-speaker diarization timestamps with standard Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) outputs, the community-1 pipeline introduces a specialized feature known as exclusive\_speaker\_diarization.2 Traditional ASR models, including Whisper, operate on the assumption of a strictly sequential, single-speaker timeline. Attempting to reconcile a rich, multi-speaker overlapping timeline with Whisper's rigid timestamp generation frequently leads to severe algorithmic alignment failures.2  
To resolve this engineering pain point, the exclusive\_speaker\_diarization mode forces a single active speaker at any given frame. It algorithmically selects the identity most likely to be transcribed by the ASR engine and actively discards the overlapping secondary voice (such as a supportive backchannel or an interruption).2 While this feature dramatically simplifies Speech-to-Text (STT) alignment and provides immense value for broad commercial transcription applications (e.g., call center analytics), it is fundamentally destructive for the discipline of oral history.  
By utilizing the exclusive mode, the pipeline actively deletes the structural conversational data that overlap-aware models are specifically designed to capture. Therefore, a production-grade oral history application must explicitly bypass the exclusive\_speaker\_diarization output stream. Developers must directly access the raw, underlying multi-speaker temporal intervals generated by the core segmentation model to ensure that no sociolinguistic data is permanently erased from the historical record.2

## **Compute Cost and Hardware Constraints: The CPU-Bound Execution Dilemma**

Deploying a state-of-the-art, overlap-aware diarization pipeline natively on a Central Processing Unit (CPU) while operating strictly under 8GB of total system memory presents an array of severe computational bottlenecks. Standard modern implementations of diarization rely heavily on the PyTorch framework, which is fundamentally architected and optimized for GPU tensor operations, high-bandwidth memory access, and massive parallelization. When forced onto CPU hardware, PyTorch models suffer from unacceptably high latency, thread contention, and excessive memory allocation.13

### **Profiling the PyTorch CPU Bottleneck**

Executing the native pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1 or the upgraded community-1 pipeline on a standard CPU exposes profound architectural inefficiencies. To quantify this, consider standard execution times: while a GPU deployment utilizing a modest Nvidia RTX 3060 can process 30 minutes of high-resolution audio in roughly 30 seconds 13, forcing the identical PyTorch model onto a modern multi-core CPU results in a massive degradation of processing speed. CPU processing times are often an order of magnitude longer, sometimes requiring up to 320 seconds for the exact same 30-minute file.13 During this prolonged execution, the PyTorch backend typically maximizes overall CPU utilization, flooding all available processing threads to perform complex matrix multiplications via Basic Linear Algebra Subprograms (BLAS) libraries.13 This creates severe contention with any other software running on the host machine.  
Furthermore, the memory footprint required to sustain the standard Pyannote pipeline in PyTorch is immense. The operational footprint is estimated at 1.5 GiB of RAM per active processing stream.16 In an edge application environment where a transcription model (Whisper), a heavy translation model (NLLB), and an entity recognition model (GLiNER) must coexist and occupy the same 8GB system pool, reserving 1.5 GiB exclusively for the diarization weights, activation tensors, and garbage collection overhead is computationally hazardous. It leaves perilously little operational overhead for the operating system, necessary file I/O operations, audio buffering, and the application's graphical user interface, practically guaranteeing out-of-memory (OOM) fatal crashes.

### **The ONNX Runtime Paradigm: Breaking the Bottleneck**

To achieve acceptable latency and fit the entire system securely within the stringent 8GB RAM constraints, the diarization backend must be migrated away from PyTorch and into the Open Neural Network Exchange (ONNX) Runtime. ONNX provides a highly standardized, deeply optimized execution environment written in C++ that aggressively strips out the dynamic computation graphs and training-specific overhead inherent to the PyTorch ecosystem.17  
The architectural advantage of leveraging ONNX for CPU execution is profound. By explicitly initializing the ONNX pipeline utilizing the CPUExecutionProvider 17, the runtime engine dynamically optimizes graph execution paths, applies mathematical weight quantization where permissible, and manages thread allocation with a degree of efficiency that PyTorch cannot match. This eliminates the Python Global Interpreter Lock (GIL) bottleneck during heavy inference phases.

#### **Memory Footprint Analysis of ONNX Artifacts**

When the complex neural components of the diarization pipeline are successfully exported to ONNX format, their requisite disk space and active RAM footprint become shockingly minimal, proving the viability of the offline edge-compute concept.  
The primary artifact is the segmentation and overlap detection model. The onnx-community/pyannote-segmentation-3.0 model, which acts as the core algorithmic engine for detecting both generic voice activity and simultaneous overlapping speech at the 16-millisecond frame level, is merely **5.99 MB** in size when exported as a standard FP32 .onnx binary.19 This is an exceptionally small payload for a model capable of highly complex acoustic differentiation.  
The secondary artifact is the speaker embedding model, responsible for extracting the unique acoustic fingerprint (voiceprint) of the speaker. State-of-the-art embedding architectures, such as the WeSpeaker ResNet34 model, are similarly optimized. The ONNX export of the WeSpeaker ResNet34 Large Margin (LM) model occupies a mere **26.5 MB** of space.21  
Loading these compiled binaries into the ONNX Runtime requires negligible initialization overhead. Combined, the static neural weights required for a complete, state-of-the-art overlap-aware pipeline require under 35 MB of base RAM.19 Even when factoring in the dynamically allocated memory required for continuous audio buffers, Short-Time Fourier Transforms (STFT), and the classical clustering matrices required for final identity assignment, the entire operational footprint of an ONNX-based diarization engine can easily remain below 200 MB.20 This complete architectural shift effectively eliminates the 1.5 GiB PyTorch memory bottleneck 16, freeing vast memory reserves for the safe, concurrent operation of Whisper and GLiNER within the 8GB limit.

### **Lightweight Algorithmic Alternatives and Specialized Frameworks**

If the standard Pyannote architecture, even when converted to ONNX, remains too heavy or structurally complex for the target application, the open-source community has rapidly developed specialized, CPU-only alternative frameworks capable of handling diarization components with extreme efficiency.  
**The diarize Library:** A recently published open-source initiative, simply named diarize, was engineered explicitly to serve as a CPU-only, highly performant alternative to Pyannote.23 Rather than relying on a heavy segmentation network, it utilizes an ultra-lightweight Silero VAD frontend. It then passes the isolated speech audio through the WeSpeaker ResNet34 ONNX embedding model, and finally applies highly optimized fast spectral clustering to group the identities.23 On an Apple M2 CPU, this specific library processes complex audio at a Real-Time Factor (RTF) of 0.12, meaning it processes audio roughly 8 times faster than real-time.23 This stands in stark contrast to Pyannote's 0.86 RTF on identical hardware.23 While the base diarize implementation currently lacks native overlap handling—enforcing a strict single-speaker-per-frame rule—its lightweight modular architecture makes it a prime candidate for modification. Developers could surgically insert the 5.99 MB ONNX-based overlap classifier immediately prior to the clustering step, yielding an incredibly fast, overlap-aware hybrid system.19  
**The sherpa-onnx Framework:** Originating from the highly respected Kaldi and Next-Gen Kaldi ecosystem, sherpa-onnx provides an incredibly lightweight, pure C++ implementation for speech-to-text, VAD, and comprehensive speaker diarization.24 Designed from the ground up for embedded systems, edge hardware like the Raspberry Pi, and offline robotics without internet access, sherpa-onnx utilizes heavily optimized C++ routines with absolutely zero Python dependencies during execution.25 While adopting this framework would require migrating the application away from its established Python-centric DiarizationEngine abstraction, the raw compute savings and ultra-low latency represent the theoretical ceiling for edge-device efficiency.25  
**The pyannote-onnx-extended Repository:** This specific repository beautifully bridges the gap between the high theoretical accuracy of Pyannote and the harsh realities of edge compute limitations.17 It is a pure ONNX runtime implementation, completely stripping out the PyTorch dependency. Crucially for oral history applications, it implements highly robust overlap handling via a custom technique termed "Average Stitching." This mathematical approach smooths overlapping speech segment probabilities across the sliding windows, preventing the jarring boundary cutoffs typical of CPU processing.17 Furthermore, it utilizes a sophisticated two-stage clustering approach: highly stable "long" audio segments are used to definitively define the speaker identities, while volatile "short" segments (which frequently represent overlapping backchannels, interruptions, or rapid laughter) are subsequently assigned to the nearest, pre-established speaker cluster.17 This framework directly satisfies all application requirements: it provides native overlap-aware processing, remains highly CPU-efficient, and is deeply lightweight.

## **Data Modeling for Simultaneous Speech: Integrating WebVTT and OHMS XML 6.0**

Achieving highly accurate, overlap-aware diarization at the neural level is computationally futile if the underlying application data model cannot structurally store, represent, or export concurrent temporal events. For a desktop application heavily focused on archival oral history, transitioning the core data schema to support simultaneous multi-speaker tracks is mandatory.

### **The Structural Limitations of Linear Segment Models**

Historically, automated transcription systems and the ASR engines that power them have assumed a strictly monophonic, linear timeline. A standard software Segment class might contain a start\_ms integer, an end\_ms integer, and a single speaker\_label string.12 In this programming paradigm, the entire audio timeline is modeled as an ordered, mutually exclusive list of non-overlapping objects.  
Consider a common oral history scenario: Speaker A is recounting a long narrative. Halfway through Speaker A's sentence, Speaker B utters a brief, affirmative backchannel ("Exactly," or "mhm") that lasts for 800 milliseconds, while Speaker A continues speaking uninterrupted. If the neural model accurately detects this overlap, the rigid, linear schema is forced into one of two destructive, lossy behaviors:

1. **Algorithmic Fragmentation:** Speaker A's continuous segment is abruptly and artificially cut off at the exact millisecond Speaker B begins. A new 800-millisecond segment is created exclusively for Speaker B. Finally, a third segment is generated to resume Speaker A's speech. This fragmentation destroys the linguistic continuity of Speaker A's sentence, severely degrading the performance of downstream Natural Language Processing (NLP) models attempting to extract semantic meaning or perform sentiment analysis.  
2. **Algorithmic Omission:** To preserve the continuity of Speaker A's narrative, Speaker B's backchannel is simply ignored, overwritten, and permanently dropped from the transcribed record.2

Neither outcome is acceptable for archival oral history, where the interaction between subjects is paramount. The required architectural shift involves deprecating the flat list of discrete temporal chunks in favor of independent, parallel speaker tracks, or utilizing a mathematical interval tree that natively tolerates temporal overlap.

### **WebVTT: The Open Standard for Simultaneous Cues**

The elegant solution to formatting, storing, and exporting multi-speaker overlapping data lies in adopting the Web Video Text Tracks (WebVTT) standard. Unlike older subtitle formats like SubRip (SRT), WebVTT was designed by the W3C to natively support the representation of simultaneous speakers and overlapping time intervals without structurally breaking the file schema.27  
In a WebVTT data structure, text cues are defined by a timestamp start, a literal \--\> string delimiter, and a precise timestamp end.28 Crucially, the specification dictates that cues may possess overlapping timings.28 When two individual cues share overlapping temporal windows, compliant video players and text parsers are instructed to render them simultaneously on screen.29 By assigning specific CSS classes, metadata identifiers, or distinct vocal styles to specific speakers (for example, utilizing the syntax \<v Speaker1\> Narrative text\</v\>), a single WebVTT text file can perfectly encapsulate the entirety of an overlapping conversation without resorting to fragmentation or omission.30

### **Compatibility with the OHMS XML 6.0 Schema**

The ultimate destination for meticulously transcribed oral history data is frequently the Oral History Metadata Synchronizer (OHMS) XML format. This specific format interfaces with institutional content management systems to provide users with highly searchable, time-synchronized transcripts connected directly to the archival audio or video.32  
Historically, the legacy OHMS XML format relied on a rudimentary synchronization method: dropping standalone timecodes at regular intervals (e.g., exactly every 30 seconds or 1 minute) into a massive, raw block of text.32 This primitive structure, defined by basic \<sync\> and \<transcript\> XML tags 33, was fundamentally incapable of mapping exact overlapping speech events at the millisecond level required by modern neural networks.  
However, recognizing the critical need for granular, sentence-level synchronization driven by AI transcription, the Louie B. Nunn Center for Oral History recently executed a major architectural update to the OHMS platform, supporting WebVTT natively. Under the new OHMS XML 6.0 schema specification, the legacy, interval-based synchronization tags are officially deprecated. In their place, the schema introduces the \<vtt\_transcript\> and \<vtt\_transcript\_alt\> XML elements.33  
This schema evolution unlocks a seamless, highly precise data pipeline for edge applications:

1. **In-Memory Representation:** The desktop application updates its internal Python Segment model to utilize interval trees, allowing lists of concurrent active speakers at any given timestamp t.  
2. **Export Transformation:** During the XML generation phase, the internal Python segments are mapped directly to standard WebVTT cues, mathematically preserving all overlapping intervals without collision.28  
3. **XML Embedding:** The entire generated WebVTT string is safely embedded within the \<vtt\_transcript\> node of the OHMS XML 6.0 document, often wrapped in a CDATA section to prevent character escaping issues.33  
4. **Viewer Rendering:** When the web-based OHMS Viewer parses the resulting XML, it utilizes standard WebVTT rendering logic, allowing the public-facing archive interface to accurately and elegantly display simultaneous text tracks.35

## **Strategic Implementation Paths for Desktop Oral History Transcription**

Given the strict 8GB RAM CPU constraint and the explicit requirement to integrate smoothly with the existing Python DiarizationEngine abstraction, migrating to a fully overlap-aware system must be handled systematically to prevent application instability. The following implementation approaches are ranked from minimal engineering effort (yielding immediate, incremental user value) to a comprehensive, high-efficiency architectural rewrite.

### **Approach 1: Incremental VAD Interception and WebVTT Schema Alignment (Minimal Effort)**

Before modifying the underlying neural backend, the application's core data structure must be structurally updated to accept simultaneous events. Attempting to force overlapping data into a linear model will result in application crashes.

* **Implementation Steps:**  
  1. Redesign the internal Segment data model to decouple strict speech boundaries from single-speaker constraints. Implement an interval tree or a parallel track architecture (e.g., maintaining an independent list of segments for Speaker\_00 and Speaker\_01).  
  2. Update the application's OHMS XML exporter to output valid OHMS XML 6.0 via the \<vtt\_transcript\> tag, mapping the existing segments into standard, overlapping WebVTT cues.33  
  3. Retain the currently deployed pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1 or community-1 pipeline. However, intercept the raw, multi-class probabilities emitted from the underlying segmentation module *prior* to the final clustering phase.4 If the raw tensor indicates two active speakers crossing the confidence threshold at frame n, emit two separate overlapping segments to the new data model, rather than relying on the pipeline's final, forced single-speaker output.  
* **Estimated RAM and CPU Impact:** Minimal impact compared to the current baseline. The neural weights loaded into memory are identical to the current implementation. Memory utilization remains dangerously high (retaining the \~1.5 GiB PyTorch overhead) 16, but data integrity is vastly improved. CPU execution time remains high.  
* **User Value:** This establishes foundational structural compatibility with OHMS XML 6.0. Overlapping segments are no longer silently deleted during the translation phase, ensuring that vital historical backchannels and cross-talk are successfully written to the output XML file, even if the diarization processing remains slow.

### **Approach 2: Pyannote-ONNX Integration (Recommended Intermediate Step)**

To definitively resolve the compute bottleneck and secure the application within the 8GB RAM envelope without breaking the established DiarizationEngine abstraction, the system must forcefully abandon PyTorch inference in favor of the ONNX runtime.

* **Implementation Steps:**  
  1. Integrate the pyannote-onnx 18 or pyannote-onnx-extended 17 libraries directly into the Python backend.  
  2. Replace the massive, multi-gigabyte PyTorch weight files currently distributed with the application with their optimized, compiled .onnx counterparts. Specifically, deploy the segmentation-3.0.onnx model (5.99 MB) 19 and an ONNX-exported embedding model such as WeSpeaker ResNet34 (26.5 MB).22  
  3. Configure the ONNX Runtime session to initialize using the CPUExecutionProvider. Implement appropriate thread locking and thread pool limits to prevent the diarization process from starving the concurrent Whisper ASR process of CPU cycles.17  
  4. Leverage the mathematical "Average Stitching" technique found in pyannote-onnx-extended to ensure that the sliding windows accurately and smoothly classify overlapping boundaries without artifacting.17  
* **Estimated RAM and CPU Impact:** Massive optimization. The total system memory footprint allocated for diarization plummets from 1.5 GiB down to a highly stable zone under 200 MB.16 CPU latency drops dramatically due to optimized graph execution, allowing the application to process audio multiple times faster than real-time.23  
* **User Value:** This approach completely satisfies all physical constraints of the desktop edge environment. It frees up gigabytes of vital RAM, drastically accelerates overall transcription time, and utilizes highly accurate Pyannote 3.0/4.0 segmentation models to detect conversational overlap naturally and accurately.

### **Approach 3: Two-Stage Pipeline with Independent Overlap Detection (Advanced Customization)**

If global clustering algorithms remain unreliable or exhibit high confusion rates when fed overlapping acoustic embeddings, the application architecture can be modified to physically separate the algorithmic tasks of overlap detection and speaker identification.

* **Implementation Steps:**  
  1. Implement an ultra-lightweight, standalone overlap detector designed to directly process the raw audio waveform, entirely decoupled from the primary embedding extractor.8  
  2. When the standalone detector flags a high-confidence overlapping region (for instance, yielding a probability \> 0.6), the region's timestamps are extracted, logged, and temporarily removed from the main processing stream.39  
  3. A fast, highly efficient CPU clustering engine (such as the spectral clustering implementation found in the diarize Python package) runs solely on the pristine, non-overlapping segments to establish pure, high-confidence speaker identity clusters.23  
  4. The previously isolated overlapping segments are then processed through a secondary heuristic phase—comparing the acoustic features of the overlapping frames against the mathematically pure clusters established in step 3—to assign dual, overlapping labels accurately.  
* **Estimated RAM and CPU Impact:** Highly efficient. Independent ONNX-based VAD and Overlap detectors are computationally trivial to execute.39 The CPU is spared the massive mathematical effort of applying complex spectral clustering to entangled, noisy audio frames, resulting in lower thermal throttling and reduced power draw.  
* **User Value:** This approach yields the highest possible diarization accuracy mathematically attainable for a constrained edge device. By actively preventing overlapping frames from poisoning the primary clustering algorithm, speaker confusion is minimized to near zero, resulting in pristine, publication-ready Oral History transcripts.

### **Approach 4: Total C++ Migration via Sherpa-ONNX (Maximum Efficiency, Highest Effort)**

For absolute maximum processing performance on low-end or aging hardware, the application developers can choose to circumvent Python orchestration entirely for the heavy diarization task.

* **Implementation Steps:**  
  1. Deprecate the Python-based DiarizationEngine class structure.  
  2. Implement sherpa-onnx as a statically compiled C++ binary, running as a discrete subprocess managed by the main application.24  
  3. Pass the raw audio stream from the application to the sherpa-onnx binary via local memory buffers or standard input/output pipes to avoid disk read/write latency.  
  4. The C++ engine utilizes deeply optimized Next-Gen Kaldi logic to handle VAD, diarization, and overlap natively, returning a structured JSON payload containing the precise, overlapping timestamps to the main application for WebVTT formatting.25  
* **Estimated RAM and CPU Impact:** This represents the theoretical minimum memory footprint and the lowest absolute CPU cycle cost possible for this mathematical operation.25 Memory usage drops to sub-100MB levels, and execution is lightning-fast, entirely decoupled from Python's restrictive Global Interpreter Lock (GIL).  
* **User Value:** This radical approach allows the application to run smoothly and rapidly on hardware vastly inferior to the stated 8GB target. It heavily future-proofs the application for highly constrained, remote field deployments (e.g., utilizing aging legacy laptops for field archiving in remote locations without internet access). However, the engineering development effort required to rewrite the abstraction layer and handle inter-process communication is substantial.

## **Conclusion and Recommended Architectural Approach**

The requirement to perform accurate, overlap-aware speaker diarization entirely offline on CPU hardware constrained to less than 8GB of RAM represents a formidable engineering challenge. The intrinsic acoustic complexity of oral history—characterized by rapid turn-taking, structural backchannels, and simultaneous speech—renders traditional single-speaker diarization models obsolete and actively destructive to the archival record.  
For a desktop transcription application where two to three overlapping speakers are the statistical norm, **Approach 2 (Pyannote-ONNX Integration coupled with WebVTT Schema Alignment)** is the unequivocal recommendation.  
This specific architectural approach strikes the optimal, pragmatic balance between necessary engineering effort and required computational performance. By retaining the core Python DiarizationEngine abstraction, the development team minimizes codebase disruption and accelerates time-to-market. Migrating the underlying heavy tensor operations away from PyTorch and into the ONNX Runtime elegantly and decisively solves the 8GB RAM constraint. It reduces the memory footprint from a highly unstable 1.5 GiB down to a safe, highly manageable operating zone of \~100-200 MB, ensuring peaceful coexistence with concurrent processes like Whisper and GLiNER. By exclusively utilizing the optimized 5.99 MB segmentation-3.0.onnx model, the system successfully retains the world-class, frame-level overlap detection capabilities inherent to the Pyannote architecture without inheriting its computational bloat.  
Finally, updating the internal data schema to an overlapping interval track model and rendering the final output via the OHMS XML 6.0 \<vtt\_transcript\> tag ensures that no crucial sociological or linguistic data is lost during the export phase. The vital interruptions, cross-talk, and affirmative backchannels that form the structural heartbeat of human conversation are meticulously preserved with millisecond precision. This architectural synthesis ensures that the software acts as a true, high-fidelity digital archivist, capturing the full depth of the human experience.
