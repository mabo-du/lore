# Gap Durations Over Overlap Flags: A Minimalist Approach to Improving Local LLM Summarization with Timing Data

## Theoretical Foundations and Benchmarks for Timing-Enhanced Summarization

The hypothesis that enriching conversational transcripts with temporal data improves summarization quality is grounded in a substantial body of evidence from multiple disciplines, including linguistics, cognitive science, and computer science. While direct benchmarks specifically testing this hypothesis with a local 1.5B language model are not available in the provided sources, an analysis of relevant datasets and foundational research strongly supports the premise. Conversational turn-taking is a highly structured system governed by rules that universally avoid long silences and overlapping speech [[95](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2705608/), [154](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/278036944_Timing_in_turn-taking_and_its_implications_for_processing_models_of_language)]. These structural elements—gaps, overlaps, and pauses—are not merely artifacts but are critical carriers of pragmatic and emotional meaning [[94](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/222695431_Pauses_gaps_and_overlaps_in_conversations)]. For instance, the duration of silence between speakers' turns is a fundamental cue that reflects cognitive states, such as knowledge or hesitation, and can signal agreement or disagreement [[31](https://www.mdpi.com/2226-471X/8/1/26)]. Research has shown that brief pauses during collaborative dialogues can enhance perceived helpfulness by eliciting verbal assents and signaling thoughtfulness [[28](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/397156723_The_power_of_pausing_in_collaborative_conversations), [30](https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jobhdp/v191y2025ics0749597825000676.html)]. Conversely, longer or more frequent silences can indicate disengagement, dominance, or conflict [[33](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10102974/)]. An LLM processing a flat transcript stripped of this information is deprived of these primary signals, which impairs its ability to reason about the true nature of the interaction, such as distinguishing a patient interview from a cross-examination or a collaborative brainstorming session from an adversarial debate.

Several benchmark datasets provide a proxy for evaluating this problem domain, confirming its relevance in academic and industrial research. MeetingBank is a particularly salient dataset, comprising transcripts from city council meetings, which are inherently formal and deliberative conversations where understanding turn-taking dynamics is crucial for accurate summarization [[6](https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.906.pdf), [7](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/372916005_MeetingBank_A_Benchmark_Dataset_for_Meeting_Summarization), [11](https://zenodo.org/records/7989108)]. Similarly, MEDIASUM contains media interviews, a domain rich with nuanced conversational exchanges that could benefit significantly from timing cues [[164](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/MediaSum_NAACL-1.pdf)]. QMSum, another key benchmark, focuses on query-based meeting summarization across multiple domains [[10](https://opendatalab.com/OpenDataLab/QMSum), [35](https://aclanthology.org/2021.naacl-main.472.pdf)]. One source provides compelling quantitative evidence directly supporting the research goal, noting that a proposed system incorporating timing-aware features achieved improvements of +0.42 on Rouge-L and +0.34 on BertScore on the QMSum benchmark [[34](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.0324)]. This finding is the closest available piece of direct evidence demonstrating that timing enrichment can lead to measurable gains in summarization quality according to standard automatic metrics. Although these benchmarks do not perform the exact ablation study requested (comparing a local 1.5B model with and without timing features), they validate the task and suggest positive outcomes. The existence of specialized benchmarks like TimE, designed to evaluate the temporal reasoning capabilities of LLMs, further indicates that this area is an active and recognized field of inquiry, suggesting that models possess latent capabilities related to time that can be leveraged through appropriate prompting [[101](https://openreview.net/forum?id=su3Ch8Ia4a)].

## Comparative Analysis of Timing Feature Representations

The method used to inject timing data into a textual prompt is a critical factor determining both the effectiveness and feasibility of the approach, especially under the strict constraints of a local 1.5B model running on a CPU. The user proposed several representation strategies, each with distinct trade-offs in terms of information density, token consumption, and clarity for the model. The primary options are inline tags, structured preludes, and other less common formats. The literature suggests that for resource-constrained environments, simplicity and efficiency are paramount.

Inline tagging, such as inserting markers like `<gap=2.3s>` between conversational turns or `<overlap>` at points of simultaneous speech, offers a highly efficient and unambiguous way to present timing data. This format adds minimal token overhead while preserving the granular, turn-by-turn structure of the conversation. By placing the tag immediately after a speaker segment, it creates a strong contextual link between the timing event and the surrounding dialogue, allowing the model to associate the pause or overlap directly with the preceding and succeeding utterances. This direct association is likely to be more effective than abstract statistical summaries. Furthermore, this approach is straightforward to implement programmatically and does not require complex parsing by the model. The GGUF format, popularized by the 'llama.cpp' toolchain, is designed for efficient local inference and often involves post-training quantization (PTQ) of model weights, making it well-suited for such additive, text-based augmentations [[102](https://arxiv.org/html/2605.00519v2), [133](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.14277v1)]. Inline tags align perfectly with this paradigm, acting as simple, structured metadata embedded within the natural language flow.

In contrast, using a structured prelude—a paragraph at the beginning of the prompt that summarizes timing statistics—is a less effective strategy for this specific use case. For example, a prelude stating, "This conversation has 142 turns from 2 speakers with an average gap of 0.8s and 12 interruption events" provides high-level, aggregated information. While potentially useful for a coarse-grained understanding, it abstracts away the fine-grained, turn-level dynamics that are essential for capturing the ebb and flow of a conversation. The variance in gap durations, the sequence of short and long pauses, and the precise moments of overlap contain far richer information than simple averages. This statistical summary also consumes a larger block of tokens for the preamble, reducing the amount of actual transcript that can be included within the model's context window. Given that smaller models are often more sensitive to prompt length and complexity, this token expenditure may yield a poor return on investment [[82](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.00641v2)]. Other formats discussed in the literature involve more complex representations, such as embeddings or structured data hierarchies, but these are generally unsuitable for a local GGUF model, which relies on text-based inputs and lacks the infrastructure for multimodal or non-textual data processing [[70](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/404350445_A_survey_on_edge_multimodal_large_models_compression_inference_acceleration_and_applications), [98](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11339515/), [150](https://research.nvidia.com/labs/lpr/storm/)]. Therefore, based on the need for low token cost, high information density, and preservation of conversational structure, inline tagging emerges as the superior representation strategy.

## Identifying the Minimum Viable and Most Impactful Timing Feature

Under the strict constraints of a local 1.5B model with a limited context window, the goal is to identify the single most impactful timing feature that provides the highest improvement in summary quality per token of overhead. This requires a careful analysis of candidate features—namely, gap durations, speaker-switch frequency, and overlapping speech markers—based on their theoretical importance, token cost, and the complexity of the signal they convey. The optimal choice must balance maximum informational value with minimal cognitive load on the model.

Gap durations, represented as `<gap=X.Ys>`, stand out as the most promising candidate. The length of silence between speakers is a fundamental and universal aspect of turn-taking systems across languages, serving as a primary indicator of conversational momentum and participant intent [[42](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10077995/), [95](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2705608/)]. Short gaps often signify rapid, fluid, and engaged discussion, while longer gaps can imply contemplation, disagreement, or even disengagement [[31](https://www.mdpi.com/2226-471X/8/1/26)]. Research has demonstrated that even sub-second pauses can be beneficial in collaborative settings, enhancing perceived helpfulness [[28](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/397156723_The_power_of_pausing_in_collaborative_conversations)]. This feature provides a continuous, quantitative signal that allows for a nuanced understanding of the conversation's dynamic. For example, a series of one-second gaps might suggest a thoughtful but productive exchange, whereas a five-second gap could signal a point of contention. The token cost for this feature is extremely low; a tag like `<gap=2.3s>` is only a few characters, making it highly efficient. For a 1.5B model, which can struggle with overly complex prompts due to "prompt noise," this simple, repetitive format is likely to be processed effectively [[82](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.00641v2), [83](https://aclanthology.org/2025.naacl-long.253/)].

Speaker-switch frequency is a higher-level, statistical feature that measures how often speakers change roles. While informative, it is a coarser-grained metric. A high frequency might imply a chaotic or contentious discussion, while a low frequency could suggest a monologue or deep consensus. However, this metric loses all the turn-by-turn detail contained in individual gap and overlap events. It provides a snapshot rather than a movie of the conversation. Its token cost is also higher than that of inline tags, as it requires a separate sentence or paragraph to state the statistic, consuming valuable context window space [[44](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3716553.3750755)].

Overlapping speech markers, such as the `<overlap>` tag, represent a powerful but binary signal. Overlapping talk is generally avoided in conversation and its presence often signifies strong emotion, conflict, enthusiasm, or a breakdown of conversational norms [[95](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2705608/)]. This makes it a very salient feature for identifying high-stakes or emotionally charged moments. The token cost is negligible. However, its primary limitation is that it provides no information about the duration or intensity of the overlap. A brief interruption is semantically different from a prolonged argumentative overlap, yet both would be marked identically. This lack of granularity means it captures less of the subtle dynamics that define a conversation compared to gap duration.

Based on this comparative analysis, gap duration emerges as the single most impactful feature to implement first. It offers the best combination of high theoretical importance, rich quantitative information, extremely low token cost, and suitability for the target model's capabilities. It directly addresses the core hypothesis by providing the model with a continuous signal about conversational pace, which is critical for inferring the underlying sentiment and dynamics of the interaction. Implementing `<gap>` tags represents the lowest-risk, highest-reward starting point for improving summarization quality.

| Feature | Token Cost | Information Richness | Suitability for 1.5B Model |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Gap Duration (`<gap=X.Ys>`) [[42](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10077995/)]** | Very Low | High (Continuous, Quantitative) | Excellent |
| **Overlap Marker (`<overlap>`) [[95](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2705608/)]** | Very Low | Medium (Binary) | Good |
| **Speaker-Switch Frequency** | Moderate | Low (Statistical, Coarse) | Fair |

## A Practical Offline Evaluation Framework for Local Models

Given the project's constraints and the absence of a dedicated human-annotated benchmark for timing-enriched summarization, a robust, fully automated, and offline evaluation framework is essential. The primary challenge lies in obtaining reliable reference summaries against which to compare the model's outputs. The recommended methodology combines established automatic metrics with a pragmatic approach to reference generation, enabling a rigorous ablation study to quantify the impact of the added timing feature. This framework ensures that the evaluation process is repeatable, objective, and entirely feasible on a local machine.

The foundation of the evaluation rests on two widely accepted automatic metrics: ROUGE and BERTScore. ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) measures the lexical overlap between the generated summary and a set of reference summaries, using variants like ROUGE-1 (unigram overlap), ROUGE-2 (bigram overlap), and ROUGE-L (longest common subsequence) [[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.19339v2), [52](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/224890821_ROUGE_A_Package_for_Automatic_Evaluation_of_summaries)]. BERTScore, in contrast, evaluates semantic similarity by computing the cosine similarity between the contextual embeddings of tokens in the candidate and reference sentences, making it better at capturing paraphrasing and synonym usage [[64](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.09675), [65](https://pypi.org/project/bert-score/0.3.1/)]. Using both metrics provides a comprehensive view of both lexical accuracy and semantic fidelity. Python libraries such as `rouge-score` and `bert-score` make the implementation of these metrics straightforward [[90](https://pypi.org/project/rouge-score/), [91](https://pypi.org/project/bert-score/0.2.2/)].

The most critical step is generating a consistent and high-quality set of reference summaries. Since the original datasets like MeetingBank may not have been annotated with summaries for enriched transcripts, a pragmatic solution is required. The most effective approach is to generate a new set of reference summaries using a powerful, external Large Language Model (e.g., GPT-4 via an API). This involves taking the original, un-enriched transcripts from the benchmark dataset and prompting a state-of-the-art model to generate summaries. These newly generated summaries serve as a stable target for comparison across all experimental conditions. This method ensures high quality and avoids bias from the baseline model's own summarization tendencies. An alternative, validated approach for Small Language Models (SLMs) is to use an LLM-generated summary as the reference [[82](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.00641v2)]. In this scenario, you could generate the reference summaries using the same Qwen 2.5 model on the base transcripts, then compare its output against the outputs of the enriched versions. This ensures consistency within the model family and has been shown to correlate strongly with human evaluations for SLMs, achieving a Kendall correlation coefficient of 1 in one study [[82](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.00641v2)].

With the reference summaries prepared, a controlled ablation study can be conducted to measure the impact of the gap duration feature.
1.  **Control Group:** The Qwen 2.5 1.5B model is prompted with the original, flat transcripts from the dataset. The summaries generated from this group form the baseline performance metric.
2.  **Experimental Group:** The same transcripts are pre-processed by inserting the `<gap=X.Ys>` tags between speaker turns. The model is then prompted with these enriched transcripts, and the resulting summaries are collected.
3.  **Metric Calculation:** For each summary in both the control and experimental groups, calculate the ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L, and BERTScore values against the shared set of reference summaries.
4.  **Statistical Analysis:** Compare the average metric scores of the experimental group to those of the control group. A statistically significant increase in scores for the enriched transcripts would provide strong evidence that the timing feature improves summarization quality. Reporting both absolute and relative improvements will quantify the magnitude of the effect. This systematic framework allows for a definitive, data-driven answer to the research question within the project's constraints.

## Final Recommendation: Implementation Strategy for a 1.5B LLM

Based on a comprehensive analysis of the provided literature and the specific constraints of the research goal, this report delivers a concrete, actionable strategy for investigating and implementing timing-enriched summarization with a local 1.5B Qwen 2.5 GGUF model. The central recommendation is to prioritize the addition of gap duration information as the single most impactful timing feature to implement first. This approach is supported by its high informational value, extremely low token cost, and alignment with the known conversational dynamics that LLMs can leverage to improve their understanding of dialogue.

The following table synthesizes the key recommendations for implementation:

| Component | Recommendation | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Timing Feature** | **Gap Durations (`<gap=X.Ys>`) Tags** | Provides a continuous, quantitative signal about conversational pace, which is a fundamental aspect of turn-taking dynamics [[95](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2705608/)]. It offers high information richness at a very low token cost, making it ideal for a constrained 1.5B model [[82](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.00641v2)]. |
| **Representation Format** | **Inline Tags** | Embedding tags like `<gap=1.5s>` directly after speaker turns preserves conversational structure and minimizes token overhead. This format is simple and efficient for local inference on a GGUF model [[133](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.14277v1)]. |
| **Evaluation Methodology** | **Automated Ablation Study** | Use ROUGE and BERTScore to compare summaries from un-enriched and enriched transcripts. Generate a consistent set of reference summaries using a powerful external LLM (e.g., GPT-4) to ensure a stable evaluation target [[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.19339v2), [82](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.00641v2)]. |
| **Prompt Template** | Adapted Qwen Chat Template | Structure the prompt clearly to delineate the instruction, the enriched transcript, and the desired output format. |

To execute this strategy, the following steps should be taken:

First, implement the data preprocessing step to augment the conversation transcripts. For each turn in the transcript, calculate the duration of the silence since the previous speaker finished talking. Insert an inline tag of the form `<gap=X.Ys>` immediately following the speaker label and text. This transforms a flat transcript into a temporally aware one with minimal additional tokens.

Second, adopt the recommended prompt template. This structured format helps the model understand its task and the nature of the input data. The Qwen chat template syntax provides a good model for this structure, ensuring clarity and predictability [[104](https://qwen.readthedocs.io/en/latest/getting_started/concepts.html)].

Third, establish the offline evaluation framework. Obtain a suitable dataset such as MeetingBank or QMSum [[6](https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.906.pdf), [10](https://opendatalab.com/OpenDataLab/QMSum)]. Generate a master set of reference summaries using a powerful external LLM to maintain consistency throughout the evaluation. Then, run the Qwen 2.5 model on both the original (control) and the `<gap>`-enriched (experimental) transcripts to produce two sets of summaries.

Finally, conduct the ablation study by calculating ROUGE and BERTScore metrics for both sets of generated summaries against the shared reference set. A thorough analysis of the results will determine whether the inclusion of gap durations leads to a statistically significant improvement in summary quality. This methodical approach provides a rigorous, repeatable, and entirely offline pathway to answering the core research question and identifying the most effective minimal-viable enhancement for conversation summarization.
