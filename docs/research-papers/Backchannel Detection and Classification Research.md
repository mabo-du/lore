# **Acoustic and Lexical Classification of Conversational Backchannels: Evaluating Automated Detection and Data Representation Strategies**

## **1\. The Interactional Matrix of Conversational Backchannels**

In spontaneous human conversation, particularly within dyadic interactions such as oral history interviews and task-oriented dialogues, communication is rarely a strictly sequential exchange of isolated monologues. Instead, it is a highly collaborative, continuously negotiated process mediated by an intricate array of non-intrusive listener responses. These short, pervasive vocalizations—ranging from non-lexical sounds such as "mhm," "uh-huh," and "mm," to brief lexical tokens like "yeah," "right," "okay," and "sure"—are known in conversational analysis and computational linguistics as backchannels.1 First formally conceptualized by Yngve (1970) and extensively modeled in subsequent research on conversational dynamics, backchannels serve as the fundamental structural glue of social interaction.3 They allow the current listener to signal active attention, demonstrate comprehension, express varying degrees of agreement, or convey affective states such as surprise and impatience, all without asserting a claim to the conversational floor or interrupting the primary speaker's narrative flow.1  
Within the specific context of oral history transcription and conversation analysis, the preservation of backchannels is paramount. The presence, timing, and acoustic contour of these listener responses carry significant structural and social information regarding the relationship, power dynamics, and rapport between the interviewer and the interviewee. Stripped of backchannels, a transcript loses critical metadata about the interactional alignment of the participants, fundamentally flattening a dynamic socio-linguistic exchange into a sterile, disjointed sequence of isolated statements.  
Currently, state-of-the-art automated speech processing architectures face profound challenges in detecting and categorizing these brief interactional events. Conventional Voice Activity Detection (VAD) algorithms and turn-taking models natively treat periods of overlapping speech as competitive interruptions, anomalous noise, or false starts, frequently misclassifying the communicative intent.6 Within the specific processing pipeline of Lore's system, the AudioClassifyWorker effectively detects broad, highly distinct non-verbal events such as laughter and crying via the deployment of YAMNet.8 However, it does not currently isolate or classify the nuanced continuum of short listener vocalizations. While the timing data for these events is implicitly present within the system's timestamped overlapping speech regions, the semantic and pragmatic classification of these micro-segments remains unresolved.  
This report delivers a comprehensive, expert-level analysis designed to address the architectural and machine-learning requirements for integrating backchannel detection into the AudioClassifyWorker. It systematically evaluates whether backchannels can be reliably distinguished from short, substantive conversational turns using purely acoustic features. It rigorously examines the viability of relying solely on the text and duration outputs of foundational automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems like OpenAI's Whisper, comparing rule-based heuristics against learned acoustic classifiers. Furthermore, the analysis investigates the acoustic and prosodic parameters necessary to classify the pragmatic function of these backchannels—differentiating basic continuers from markers of surprise or impatience. Finally, the report outlines a highly optimized, Central Processing Unit (CPU)-bound architectural implementation strategy, including precise model benchmarks, memory footprints, and a proposed multi-tiered JSON data model inspired by the NaturalTurn paradigm to accurately represent these events in downstream exports.

## **2\. Lexical Rule-Based Systems and the Limitations of Foundational ASR**

To address the missing classification of short listener vocalizations, one highly intuitive approach is to rely entirely on the output of the existing foundational ASR model. Under this paradigm, a rule-based system could theoretically flag any transcribed utterance with a duration of less than 800 milliseconds whose textual output matches a predefined vocabulary of filler words (e.g., {"mhm", "uh-huh", "yeah", "right", "okay"}). This text-dependent heuristic assumes that modern ASR systems faithfully and consistently transcribe all spoken audio, including non-lexical sounds and disfluencies. However, an exhaustive analysis of the architecture, training data, and processing logic of state-of-the-art ASR systems, specifically OpenAI's Whisper, reveals fundamental systemic biases that render a purely rule-based approach entirely insufficient for reliable backchannel detection.

### **2.1 The Algorithmic Erasure of Disfluencies and Filler Words**

Whisper is an end-to-end transformer-based system trained on approximately 680,000 hours of weakly supervised data pulled from internet audio and diverse multimedia sources.10 Due to the inherent nature of this training corpus—which heavily features clean prose, professional broadcast subtitles, and standardized text—the model possesses a profound bias toward generating fluent, grammatically cohesive, and syntactically standard speech.10 During its text standardization phase, Whisper actively penalizes, filters out, and suppresses non-lexical conversational elements that it deems to be formatting errors, hesitation markers, or irrelevant noise.12  
The model's official text normalization parameters, documented extensively in its architectural specifications (specifically Appendix C of the Whisper paper), explicitly mandate the removal of disfluencies. The preprocessing logic targets and algorithmically deletes words such as "hmm", "mm", "mhm", "mmm", "uh", and "um".12 Consequently, relying on a strict text-matching rule will inevitably result in critically high false-negative rates.12 The targeted backchannel sounds simply do not manifest in the final transcription payload; they are actively excised by the model, leaving either literal gaps in the timeline or silent voids where the listener response actually occurred.11  
While it is technically possible to modify the Whisper processor by passing the normalize=False parameter or manipulating the remove\_disfluencies option to skip the final text standardization step, this modification only partially mitigates the issue.10 The model's latent representation remains heavily skewed against disfluent speech patterns. Studies examining Whisper's performance on spontaneous, disfluent speech (such as stuttering or heavily conversational, unscripted dialogue) reveal that Word Error Rates (WER) are often three to four times higher for these segments compared to standard, fluent speech.14 Because the model has insufficient exposure to the phonetic topology of isolated backchannels within its training distribution, it fundamentally fails to tokenize them reliably, routinely dropping the segments entirely during inference.11

### **2.2 Short-Segment Hallucinations and Contextual Failure**

A secondary, yet equally critical, limitation of relying exclusively on Whisper for backchannel detection involves the model's auto-regressive behavior when processing highly isolated, sub-second audio frames. The Whisper architecture is designed to process audio in standardized 30-second windows.16 The model's self-attention mechanisms rely heavily on surrounding linguistic context to accurately predict the next token.17 When an ASR system encounters a very brief, isolated utterance (e.g., a 400-millisecond overlap containing the sound "uh-huh") separated from a broader conversational context, it loses the cross-attention anchors necessary for accurate decoding.6  
This acute lack of contextual grounding frequently induces transcription hallucinations.18 The auto-regressive decoder, forced to output a prediction for a localized acoustic event, will frequently misinterpret non-speech sounds, breath noises, microphone handling artifacts, or faint background chatter as filler words, markers of tone, or entirely fabricated phrases.18 In controlled evaluations of Whisper-large-v3, researchers observed that the model tends to aggressively misinterpret non-speech sounds, with a staggering 55.2% of unidentifiable audio artifacts being hallucinated into the word "so".18 Furthermore, without strict Voice Activity Detection (VAD) boundaries, Whisper is prone to entering repetitive transcription loops, generating long sequences of hallucinated text triggered by a single short noise.17 If a rule-based backchannel detector relies on Whisper's raw output for segments under 800 milliseconds, it will inevitably ingest these hallucinations, leading to massive false-positive rates that corrupt the integrity of the exported data model.

### **2.3 The Necessity of an Independent Acoustic Classifier**

The fragility of a text-dependent, rule-based heuristic becomes evident when measured against the constraints of conversational analysis. Foundational ASR models natively excise the exact lexical tokens required for the rule to trigger, while simultaneously hallucinating false tokens when fed the sub-second audio windows where backchannels reside. While there are emerging efforts to fine-tune Whisper variants specifically for verbatim transcription (such as CrisperWhisper, which enforces the detection of fillers and stutters), deploying an entirely new, heavily modified ASR model incurs significant computational overhead that violates the strict CPU deployment constraints of the AudioClassifyWorker.21  
Therefore, a lightweight, learned acoustic classifier—operating entirely independently of the ASR text output layer—is structurally required to accurately detect backchannels. A purely acoustic approach bypasses the lexical tokenization bottleneck entirely, evaluating the raw acoustic physics of the audio segment to determine conversational intent, thus ensuring high-fidelity classification regardless of whether the ASR successfully transcribes the filler word.

## **3\. Acoustic Features for Binary Classification: Backchannels vs. Substantive Speech**

Having established the necessity of a learned approach, the primary research question must be addressed: Can backchannels be reliably distinguished from short substantive turns using acoustic features alone? Extensive empirical research utilizing large-scale conversational corpora, including the Switchboard Dialogue Act (SwDA) corpus, the ICSI meeting corpus, and the AMI dataset, provides a definitive affirmative response. Lightweight classifiers relying exclusively on localized acoustic and prosodic features consistently achieve robust binary classification accuracies (backchannel versus non-backchannel) ranging from 75% to over 85%, significantly outperforming naive duration-based heuristics.22

### **3.1 The Acoustic Signatures of Interactional Intent**

The core acoustic distinction between a backchannel and a short substantive turn (e.g., a rapid factual answer, an interjection, or a competitive turn-taking interruption) lies in the physical signaling of interactional intent. Substantive turns represent a definitive attempt to claim the conversational floor and project new semantic information, requiring specific physiological exertion and acoustic dominance.25 Backchannels, conversely, are cooperative mechanisms specifically designed to avoid disrupting the primary speaker's auditory space.1 These differing intents manifest across several quantifiable acoustic parameters:

* **Fundamental Frequency (F0) and Pitch Synchronization:** Backchannels typically exhibit a highly synchronized, accommodated, or narrowed pitch relative to the primary speaker's immediately preceding utterance.28 The listener unconsciously matches the baseline pitch of the speaker to signal alignment. In stark contrast, competitive turn-taking attempts (substantive interruptions) almost universally display elevated F0 levels, a significantly wider pitch range, and an abrupt shift in pitch slope to audibly seize the floor and demand attention.28  
* **Acoustic Energy and Intensity Contours:** Listener responses are systematically produced at lower acoustic intensities than substantive speech.31 The root mean square (RMS) energy of the word "yeah" when intended as a passive backchannel is measurably lower than the RMS energy of "yeah" when intended to aggressively agree and take over the narrative.29 Furthermore, backchannels generally exhibit a flatter energy contour throughout their duration, lacking the sharp plosive spikes associated with the articulation of complex, multi-word substantive turns.  
* **Duration, Timing, and Harmonicity:** While both backchannels and short turns can exist comfortably under an arbitrary 800-millisecond threshold, their empirical distributions differ significantly. Statistical analyses of dyadic conversations indicate that backchannels average approximately 750 milliseconds in duration, whereas substantive short turns exhibit much higher variance and frequently push closer to a 1-second boundary before triggering subsequent linguistic expansion.33 Additionally, the harmonic-to-noise ratio (HNR) effectively differentiates the highly vocalized, resonant nature of a backchannel (the continuous voicing of "mhm" or "uh-huh") from the consonant-heavy, fricative articulation required for a short substantive response.24

### **3.2 Performance of Lightweight Classifiers on Conversational Datasets**

For deployment environments constrained by limited CPU availability and stringent memory budgets, traditional machine learning models and small-scale neural networks excel at modeling these acoustic features without requiring the massive compute overhead of deep transformer models.

Extensive benchmark testing reveals that a Support Vector Machine (SVM) leveraging Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs), delta coefficients, and basic pitch/energy contours provides an optimal convergence of computational frugality and high classification accuracy.24 Studies evaluating SVMs specifically on the Switchboard corpus report overall accuracies of 85.7% when acoustic cues are combined with basic discourse markers, and 83.5% when using purely acoustic and temporal features, entirely omitting lexical input.23  
More recent advancements in dialogue modeling have introduced continuous Voice Activity Projection (VAP) models, which demonstrate that backchannels can be predicted and classified continuously at the frame level rather than discretely at the utterance boundary.5 VAP models yield exceptional accuracy, frequently outperforming traditional baselines by 20% in complex, multi-party overlapping scenarios.38 However, VAP models require the continuous, ongoing processing of the entire audio stream to maintain a rolling context window. This introduces a persistent background latency and continuous CPU utilization penalty that fundamentally violates the strict constraints of an isolated, segment-processing worker designed to operate only when specific thresholds are triggered. Therefore, a segment-level feature extraction pipeline followed by a mathematically lightweight SVM or a small Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) remains the most architecturally sound and resource-efficient approach for post-hoc binary classification.

## **4\. Classifying Pragmatic Function: From Detection to Intent**

Beyond the binary detection of a backchannel, determining its specific pragmatic function—whether the listener is signaling basic agreement, demonstrating understanding, expressing profound surprise, or exhibiting impatience—adds a profound layer of analytical value to the transcript. Acoustic and prosodic features contain sufficient micro-variance to enable the sub-classification of these categories, though the boundaries between them require highly nuanced, multi-dimensional modeling.1

### **4.1 Acoustic Discrimination of Continuers vs. Assessments**

In the taxonomy of conversation analysis, backchannels are broadly divided into two major functional categories: "continuers" (which signal basic passive understanding and implicitly encourage the primary speaker to maintain the floor) and "assessments" (which evaluate the speaker's statement, expressing affective states such as agreement, surprise, or sympathy).41 The prosodic profiles of these pragmatic functions are highly distinct and quantifiable.  
**Agreement and Passive Continuers:** Utterances signaling simple agreement or serving as standard continuers (e.g., "mm-hm", "yeah", "uh-huh") are prosodically well-defined and stable. They are almost universally characterized by a phrase-final rising pitch.1 When a listener produces an affirmative cue word as a backchannel, it consistently exhibits a rising pitch slope at its acoustic terminus, which serves as a recognized interactional signal that the conversational floor remains open and unchallenged.1 Their acoustic intensity remains relatively low and heavily synchronized with the main speaker.  
**Surprise and Strong Assessment:** Backchannels designed to convey surprise, disbelief, or strong affective reaction (e.g., "really?", "wow", "oh") diverge significantly from the baseline profile of continuers. They exhibit a much higher maximum fundamental frequency and a significantly wider overall F0 range.43 Research indicates that the highest pitch observed in backchannels signaling surprise averages over 350 Hz, compared to the roughly 200 Hz average found in standard passive continuers.43 Additionally, functions expressing sympathy or strong surprise possess significantly longer temporal durations than standard continuers, often stretching the vocalization to emphasize the emotional reaction.44  
**Impatience and Turn-Grabbing:** When a listener transitions from active listening to impatience, backchanneling behavior shifts dynamically. The frequency rate of backchannels often increases rapidly (e.g., producing multiple staccato "yeah, yeah, yeah" tokens in rapid succession). Acoustically, the fundamental frequency drops significantly while overall intensity rises.25 This specific combination of low pitch, high intensity, and rapid repetition transitions the acoustic profile from a cooperative backchannel into a competitive overlap, functioning as a precursor to a substantive interruption.

### **4.2 The Interplay of Lexical Content and Prosodic Contours**

While acoustic features alone are highly effective at differentiating broad interactional categories (e.g., passive continuer versus high-arousal surprise), the highest theoretical accuracy in pragmatic classification is achieved when acoustic features are combined with lightweight lexical cues.23 This is because certain lexical items are inherently ambiguous in dialogue.  
For instance, the word "really" can function as a passive backchannel, a substantive information-seeking question, or an expression of intense surprise.40 Similarly, the word "right" can function as a simple continuer or a definitive claim of agreement. In these instances, semantics and prosody interact non-linearly. A classifier trained exclusively on prosody may struggle to differentiate an information-seeking question from a surprised backchannel, as both often utilize high pitch ranges and rising final contours.46  
However, when simple, one-hot encoded lexical categories (derived post-ASR) are concatenated with the acoustic embeddings, classification F-measures increase dramatically.2 Even if an ASR engine routinely misses non-lexical sounds like "uh-huh", it reliably captures distinct lexical backchannels like "really", "right", and "sure". By feeding these isolated, timestamped text tokens into a combined acoustic-lexical classifier, the system can disambiguate the intent with exceptional granularity, outputting highly accurate metadata regarding listener intent without requiring heavy computational resources.

## **5\. Architectural Implementation: Inference within CPU Constraints**

A critical constraint governing the deployment of this backchannel classification system is the absolute necessity for the solution to execute efficiently on a CPU, ideally residing securely within the existing AudioClassifyWorker. This worker is already actively processing audio streams through YAMNet (a deep neural network utilizing the MobileNetV1 architecture) to detect generalized auditory events such as laughter and crying.9 Furthermore, the worker environment operates within a strict 8GB memory budget, precluding the loading of massive language models or complex multi-modal transformer architectures alongside the existing stack.47

### **5.1 Leveraging YAMNet Embeddings via Transfer Learning**

Because the YAMNet model is already instantiated and actively running within the worker environment, introducing a completely separate, standalone acoustic feature extraction pipeline (e.g., deploying a bespoke CNN solely for backchannels or running a continuous VAP model) would be computationally redundant, architecturally inefficient, and highly likely to violate the memory constraints.  
YAMNet functions by computing a Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) on 25-millisecond windows of audio with 10-millisecond hops, mapping the resulting magnitudes to 64 distinct mel-frequency bins, and processing these matrices through a series of depthwise-separable convolutions to output scores across 521 distinct sound classes.49 More importantly for this application, the penultimate layer of the YAMNet architecture generates a rich, highly compressed 1024-dimensional latent embedding vector that captures incredibly complex spectral and temporal features of the 960-millisecond audio segment it processes.49  
Instead of training an entirely new end-to-end deep learning model from scratch, the optimal engineering strategy is to utilize **transfer learning** by intercepting these pre-computed YAMNet embeddings directly before they pass to the final softmax classification layer.52 By attaching a lightweight Support Vector Machine (SVM) or a highly compact Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) to the 1024-dimensional output, the system can classify backchannels and infer their pragmatic functions with near-zero additional computational overhead, effectively piggybacking on the heavy lifting already performed by the YAMNet convolutions.51

### **5.2 CPU Inference Benchmarks and Hardware Optimization**

Operating a small MLP or SVM strictly on top of pre-computed embeddings requires a negligible computational footprint, making it perfectly suited for the 8GB budget and CPU-only environment.

* **Memory Footprint:** The foundational YAMNet model occupies fewer than 3.7 million parameters, requiring roughly 15MB of disk space and a minimal RAM footprint during execution.48 A downstream SVM or a 2-layer MLP (e.g., scaling 1024 input nodes \-\> 128 hidden nodes \-\> 4 output classification nodes) requires less than 1MB of operational memory, effortlessly fitting within the strict 8GB ceiling.47  
* **Latency Benchmarks:** YAMNet inference on a standard modern CPU requires approximately 12 to 15 milliseconds per audio frame.55 Processing the subsequent 1024-dimensional embedding vector through an ONNX-optimized MLP or a compiled scikit-learn SVM executes in the sub-millisecond range (measured in tens of microseconds).57

To guarantee maximum throughput and prevent latency spikes, the inference module must utilize the ONNX Runtime library, explicitly configured for CPU execution utilizing the CPUExecutionProvider.59 Carefully adjusting intra-op and inter-op thread counts to match the exact physical core count of the host machine prevents thread oversubscription and context-switching penalties. This configuration ensures deterministic, ultra-low-latency execution that will not significantly impact overarching transcription speeds, block the worker queue, or violate post-hoc processing SLAs.59

### **5.3 Limitations of Generalized Embeddings and Fallback Strategies**

While mathematically elegant and architecturally efficient, it is necessary to formally acknowledge a theoretical limitation of utilizing YAMNet embeddings for highly granular prosodic analysis. YAMNet is trained predominantly on the immense AudioSet corpus to classify broad, generalized environmental sounds (e.g., dogs barking, sirens, generic speech, loud laughter).9 Its latent spatial representation is therefore highly generalized. Consequently, while it effectively and accurately separates human vocalizations from background noise, its native embeddings may lack the extreme micro-prosodic granularity required to differentiate subtle pragmatic functions—such as distinguishing a marginally rising pitch contour (indicating agreement) from a completely flat pitch contour (indicating a substantive turn) at the highest possible fidelity.48  
If empirical testing reveals that the YAMNet-MLP cascade yields sub-optimal precision for pragmatic sub-classification, an engineered fallback strategy is required. The alternative approach involves bypassing the YAMNet embeddings for this specific sub-task and computing standard Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) and F0 (pitch) traces directly from the raw audio snippet using a highly optimized, lightweight C++ or Python library (e.g., librosa or essentia). Passing a strictly bounded 30-dimensional MFCC array, coupled with an F0 slope integer and an energy delta, into a standard SVM provides state-of-the-art conversational classification.23 This fallback methodology remains significantly below the computational cost of deep convolutional networks and easily satisfies all CPU and memory constraints.

## **6\. Data Modeling and Representation of Listener Responses**

The final architectural and theoretical consideration involves determining how to best represent detected backchannels within the system's exported data model. In standard, naive transcription paradigms, backchannels are handled poorly: they are either completely discarded (resulting in the loss of interactional metadata) or they are transcribed as primary, standalone conversational turns, which creates severely fragmented, highly disjointed transcripts that frustrate researchers and analysts.65  
If Speaker A delivers a flowing 30-second narrative, and Speaker B interjects with a brief "mhm" at second 15, a standard chronological ASR system will artificially split Speaker A's continuous turn into two distinct 15-second segments, inserting Speaker B's backchannel as an independent block between them. This destroys the psychological and interactional reality of the conversation, which actually consists of one continuous narrative turn accompanied by parallel, non-intrusive secondary feedback.65

### **6.1 The NaturalTurn Paradigm**

To accurately reflect the interactional dynamics inherent in oral history and conversational interviews, the data model must adopt a multi-tier, hierarchical structure conceptually aligned with the NaturalTurn algorithm.65 The NaturalTurn paradigm explicitly solves the fragmentation problem by distinguishing a speaker's primary conversational turns from a listener's secondary utterances.66 Under this sophisticated model, backchannels and listener responses are treated as parallel acoustic events that occur *concurrently during* a primary turn, rather than as sequential events that interrupt it.66

### **6.2 Data Schema Recommendations and JSON Structuring**

Backchannels should absolutely not be stripped out into a completely separate audio channel or isolated text file, as this completely breaks the temporal alignment necessary for analyzing the dialogue's rhythm. Instead, they should remain as instantiated Segment objects, but they must be structurally subordinated and linked to the primary speaker's active turn.  
This hierarchical relationship is achieved by introducing specific metadata flags and relational mapping within the export JSON schema:

1. **Turn Role Classification:** Every identified segment in the transcript receives a fundamental turn\_type attribute, strictly categorized as either primary or secondary based on its classification.33  
2. **Backchannel Flagging:** Segments identified by the acoustic classifier as secondary listener responses receive a dedicated boolean flag: backchannel: true.69  
3. **Pragmatic Annotation:** A subsequent string field, pragmatic\_function, stores the output of the downstream classification model, enriching the segment with intent metadata (e.g., "agreement", "surprise", "continuer", "impatience").1  
4. **Temporal Nesting:** The secondary segment is nested within, or relationally mapped to, the primary segment that spans the identical timestamp window.

### **6.3 Export and User Interface Implications**

By explicitly nesting or relationally linking secondary backchannels to the overarching primary segment, downstream applications (such as qualitative coding software, NVivo, subtitling engines, or proprietary web UI timelines) can dynamically choose how to render the interactional data.27  
A sophisticated user interface could visually display the transcribed backchannel as a small, translucent marker hovering directly over the primary speaker's waveform at the exact millisecond it occurred. This perfectly preserves the structural relationship and rapport established between the interviewer and interviewee without artificially truncating the primary narrative flow, satisfying the exact analytical requirements of oral historians and linguists.6

## **7\. Synthesized Conclusions and Strategic Recommendations**

The challenge of isolating, classifying, and accurately representing conversational backchannels cannot be resolved through simplistic lexical rules applied post-hoc to foundational ASR outputs. The aggressive algorithmic normalization of disfluencies and the severe short-segment hallucination loops inherent in systems like OpenAI's Whisper mandate the deployment of dedicated, independent acoustic classifiers.12 However, this necessity must be carefully balanced against rigid CPU and memory constraints.  
To achieve optimal performance, maximize transcription throughput, and enrich the system's exported metadata, the following strategic integration path is recommended for Lore's AudioClassifyWorker:

1. **Reject ASR Dependency for Detection:** Do not rely on Whisper's text output or an 800ms duration rule for primary backchannel detection. The inherent omission rate of filler words renders this approach structurally flawed.  
2. **Leverage Existing Computation via Transfer Learning:** Do not deploy a separate, heavy acoustic feature extraction pipeline. Instead, tap into the AudioClassifyWorker's currently running processes by extracting the 1024-dimensional feature embeddings generated by the penultimate layer of the already-active YAMNet model.51  
3. **Deploy a Lightweight SVM/MLP Classifier:** Route these intercepted YAMNet embeddings through an ONNX-optimized Support Vector Machine (SVM) or a small Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) configured via CPUExecutionProvider. This architecture executes in less than one millisecond per frame, adding virtually zero latency, utilizing under 1MB of memory, and delivering robust binary classification (75%–85% accuracy) between backchannels and substantive short turns.55  
4. **Incorporate Lexical Fusion for Pragmatics:** To categorize the deeper pragmatic function of the backchannel (e.g., differentiating agreement from surprise), fuse the acoustic output with any available text tokens generated by the ASR. Rely heavily on fundamental frequency (F0) peaks, pitch slopes, and duration as the primary discriminators for interactional intent.40  
5. **Adopt the NaturalTurn Hierarchical Data Schema:** Format the final JSON output to reflect the psychological and temporal reality of conversation. Mark backchannels as parallel, secondary events (backchannel=true), strictly nested beneath the continuous primary turn of the active speaker.65

This hybrid acoustic-lexical methodology ensures the exhaustive capture of rich interactional metadata, preserving the critical relational dynamics inherent in conversational datasets without violating infrastructure constraints.
